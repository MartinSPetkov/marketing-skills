# Marketing Leadership: The 12 Powers Framework

Based on the largest global study ever conducted on marketing leadership (1,232 senior marketers + 67,278 360-degree responses + 100+ CMO interviews).

## The Core Idea: The V-Zone

The V-Zone (Value Creation Zone) is the overlap between what customers need and what the company needs. The best marketing leaders maximize this overlap.

Working inside the V-Zone creates value for customers (better products and experiences), value for the company (revenue and profit), and value for the marketer (influence and career success).

The three gaps that make marketing leadership uniquely hard:
- **Trust gap**: Most marketing work involves the future, so bosses and colleagues will always doubt it
- **Power gap**: A great customer experience involves many departments, most of which don't report to marketing
- **Skills gap**: Marketing technology changes faster than any marketer can track

The 12 Powers are the specific leadership behaviors that close these gaps.

## Section I: Mobilize Your Boss

### Power #1: Tackle Only Big Issues

**Central question**: Am I working on the topics that matter most for the V-Zone?

A big issue is one that matters a lot to both customers AND the company's top team. Marketers who talk about advertising and branding lose the C-suite. Marketers who talk about revenue, cost, profit, and customer experience gain it.

The research gap: 76% of marketers say they know what matters for the business. Only 46% of their bosses agree.

**Diagnosis questions**:
- Can you describe your work in revenue and profit terms, not marketing terms?
- Does your CEO list your projects among the company's top priorities?
- Are you spending most of your time on things that appear on the CEO's worry list?

**Practical actions**:
- List the top 5 issues keeping your CEO up at night. Cross-check your current priorities against that list.
- Reframe how you describe your work. "I manage the brand" becomes "I help grow market share by keeping us the preferred choice."
- If working at a lower level, always connect your projects to company-level metrics before presenting them upward.

---

### Power #2: Deliver Returns, No Matter What

**Central question**: Am I a cost or revenue?

CEOs think in four categories: strategy, organization, revenue, and cost. If your boss doesn't associate you with revenue, you are a cost. Costs get cut.

The research gap: Over 50% of C-suite executives in one study didn't believe their marketing spend was significantly driving top-line revenue.

**Practical actions**:

*Align on how marketing works*: Build a simple marketing model (awareness, preference, purchase) together with finance. Present it widely. Not because it's perfect but because it creates a shared language.

*Open the books*: Invite finance into a joint review of all marketing spend. Counterintuitive but effective. It builds credibility faster than any presentation.

*Give your issues a price tag*: Quantify the cost of the problem you're solving. "If we don't fix customer retention, we lose X in revenue over 3 years" lands harder than "we need to improve retention."

*Behave like an investor*: Review marketing spend the way a CFO reviews capital allocation. Ask: what return does each activity produce? Cut the ones with no case. Double what works.

*Get into the revenue camp*: Track and report on lead generation, pipeline influence, customer acquisition cost, lifetime value, and brand preference metrics that link to purchase.

---

### Power #3: Work Only with the Best

**Central question**: Who are the best, and how can I work with them?

Working with the best external partners isn't a budget question, it's a returns question. The best don't always cost the most.

Only 62% of marketing leaders in the study claimed to work with the best. Many default to local or familiar partners.

**Practical actions**:
- Read industry blogs and magazines from multiple countries to find the best campaigns and who made them.
- Attend conferences outside your home market.
- Meet new potential partners a few times a year, even if you're not switching. It calibrates your standards.
- When you find great talent, don't let political pressure stop you from working with them.

## Section II: Mobilize Your Colleagues

### Power #4: Hit the Head and the Heart

**Central question**: How can I win my colleagues' hearts and minds?

You can't tell colleagues what to do. Most people who determine the quality of the customer experience don't work in marketing. You need a story.

Stories contributed 3% to business impact but 7% to career success. A powerful vision raises marketing's internal visibility.

**The Heart-Head-How-To framework**:
- **Heart**: An inspiring, attainable vision. Paint a hopeful picture of the future that people can sign up for.
- **Head**: Credible evidence, ideally from customers. Data that makes the vision believable.
- **How-To**: Personal relevance. Tell people what they need to do and how they fit in.

**Practical actions**:
- Always speak in customer language internally. Not "our SKU count is too high" but "as a customer, finding the right product takes too long."
- Write the story before asking for anything. A compelling narrative gets colleagues moving faster than a strategy document.
- Use customer quotes and video clips to make abstract insights concrete.

---

### Power #5: Walk the Halls

**Central question**: How can I get people moving?

Mobilizing colleagues is never finished. It requires repeated, in-person, relationship-based influence, week after week. Sending one great email doesn't do it.

This power tied for the highest career success contribution (13%) in the study.

**Practical actions**:
- Identify the 5-10 people outside marketing who most influence the customer experience. Map how much time you spend with each of them.
- Schedule regular check-ins with key colleagues, not just when you need something.
- When you get pushback, listen first. Understand the concern before addressing it.
- Create cross-functional working groups where the win goes to the whole group, not just marketing.

---

### Power #6: You Go First

**Central question**: How can I visibly act to increase the V-Zone?

Tangible results are the only thing that permanently mobilize colleagues. Stories set up the movement. Visible action starts it. Being seen as a conduit for results contributed 12% to career success.

**Three steps to start a movement**:
1. Find your movement: An idea close to customers' hearts with wide organizational potential
2. Dare to go first: Run a small pilot. Prove it works before asking for broad support
3. Find first followers: Once the idea is out, focus on finding 2-3 people who want to join before broadcasting to everyone

**Practical actions**:
- Run small pilots early. A successful test beats a well-reasoned strategy document every time.
- Get to the front line regularly: call customers who complained, spend time with sales, work in the store.
- Use revenue and customer language when describing your actions, not marketing terminology.
- Let the movement attract followers by making early wins visible internally.

## Section III: Mobilize Your Team

### Power #7: Get the Mix Right

**Central question**: How can I design and build the right team to increase the V-Zone?

This is the single biggest driver of business impact (20% relative contribution). Building and aligning a team with the right skills matters more than any other power for business results.

The skills crisis: Marketing is short of analytical and executional skills (pricing, new product development, data analysis), even while conceptual and creative skills are strong. Only 49% of senior marketers in the study felt confident about their team's analytical skills.

**The skill mix challenge**:
- Conceptual/creative skills (brand positioning, marketing strategy): strong in most teams, and important
- Analytical/executional skills (pricing, new product development, customer analytics): equally important, but most teams are weak here
- Digital skills: important but over-emphasized at the expense of fundamental marketing thinking

**Practical actions**:
- Map your team's current skills against what the big V-Zone issues actually require.
- Identify the 2-3 skill gaps causing the most business harm. Fill those first.
- Move yourself from marketing expert to leader of leaders. Your job is to build a team that can tackle big issues, not to stay on top of every technical detail.
- Align your team around the big issue, not around functional domains.

---

### Power #8: Cover Them in Trust

**Central question**: How do I get my team to ask for forgiveness, not permission?

The goal is to build a tribe: a close-knit group of capable, mutually supportive people who tackle the big issue together.

Marketers score below average (compared to leaders in other functions) on managing conflict and controlling ego. Both are trust killers.

**The trust equation**: Trust = (Professionalism x Intimacy) / Ego

**Building professionalism**:
- Keep your promises. Visibly follow company policies. Be consistently on time and on deadline.
- Don't pretend to know everything. Celebrate team members' expertise openly.

**Building intimacy**:
- Share small personal things with the team.
- Ask for help publicly. Show how to present a knowledge gap without defensiveness.
- Consider structured trust-building exercises (360-degree sharing, Myers-Briggs discussions).

**Controlling ego**:
- Let other people present in important meetings, including to your boss.
- When the team succeeds, pass the credit to them explicitly.
- Make your office a resource for the team, not a status symbol.
- Manage conflict directly; don't avoid it.

---

### Power #9: Let the Outcomes Speak

**Central question**: How can I be a fair judge?

Aligning people's goals with business objectives and holding them accountable contributed 6% to business impact and 9% to career success.

The reputation problem: Compared to finance and sales leaders, marketers are consistently rated lower by bosses, peers, AND direct reports on performance management.

**Practical actions**:

*Set targets for everything, including small tasks*: Deadlines on small tasks signal that performance matters. Once habits form, you can ease back to only tracking critical items.

*Follow up on every deadline, consistently*: Forgotten deadlines destroy performance culture faster than anything else.

*Replace meeting minutes with action tables*: During each meeting, capture only owner, action, and deadline. Distribute by end of day.

*Create fact-based reviews*: Build regular, structured reviews where all key metrics are visible. Use traffic lights (green/red) to make status obvious at a glance. Publicly recognize people who surface problems early.

*Build a merit culture*: Base recognition and compensation on real results. Don't protect poor performers because you like them.

## Section IV: Mobilize Yourself

### Power #10: Fall in Love with Your World

**Central question**: How can I inspire others with my expertise?

Knowledge contributed 18% to business impact (the second highest of all 12 powers) and 9% to career success.

The paradox: Customer and market knowledge drives business impact. Product knowledge drives career success. You need both, but for different reasons.

The reputation problem: Only 32% of senior executives name their top marketer as the "voice of the customer" in their company.

**Practical actions**:

*Become a customer insider*:
- Spend your first weeks in any new role outside the marketing department
- Have regular direct customer conversations, not just through research
- Use your own products and competitors' products under real conditions
- Record customer conversations (with permission) and share clips internally

*Know the market*:
- Track the 3-5 structural forces reshaping your market over the next 3 years
- Know your competitors' strategies at least as well as their marketing

*Know the products*:
- Spend time with the people who build and sell your products
- Be fluent in product language; it earns credibility with every non-marketing leader

---

### Power #11: Know How You Inspire

**Central question**: How can I leverage what makes me tick?

Self-awareness about your own inspirational qualities contributed 12% to career success (the second highest of all 12 powers for career).

The Eros-Logos tension: Marketers naturally lean toward Eros (creative, emotional, relationship-oriented, outward-looking). Most of the organization runs on Logos (process, facts, numbers, precision). Knowing where you sit on this spectrum helps you adapt your inspiration style without losing your authentic strengths.

**Three steps**:

*Step 1: Confirm what makes you tick*

Answer these questions in writing:
- What drove the big decisions that got me to become a marketing leader?
- What were the happiest moments in my career?
- What most excites me in my current role?

Do the "Why, why, why" exercise: Write 6-10 roles you have in life. For each, write 6-10 reasons why it matters to you. Find the 3-5 themes that appear across multiple roles. These are your core motivators.

*Step 2: Find out how you inspire others today*

Ask 5-10 colleagues and friends directly: "How do I inspire you?" Keep it anonymous if possible. Read old performance reviews. Consider a 360-degree assessment.

*Step 3: Develop your effective authenticity*

Don't try to fake inspiration. If you don't care about something, it shows. Find the genuine connection between your motivators and the work at hand. Then lead from that place.

---

### Power #12: Aim Higher

**Central question**: What's your marketing leadership vision?

Having an inspiring personal vision contributed 5% to business impact and 13% to career success (tied for highest career impact with Walk the Halls).

A personal vision does two things: it sustains your energy during the long grind of expanding the V-Zone, and it gives other people something to follow.

**Write your Marketing Leadership Manifesto**

A written manifesto helps you commit to specific goals, not just aspirations. Plan 45 minutes alone to write it. Cover:
- Where do you want to be in 5 years?
- What difference will you have made to customers and the industry?
- What impact will you have had on the business, its culture, and its results?
- Where will you be in your career?
- How will work integrate with your personal life and priorities?

Write it however works for you: story, bullet points, free-form. The format doesn't matter. The specificity does.

Share it with someone you trust. Articulating your vision to another person sharpens it.

## Coaching Mode: Diagnosing Where to Focus

When a marketing leader wants to improve, use this prioritization logic from the research:

**Highest business impact powers** (in order): #7 Get the Mix Right (20%), #10 Fall in Love with Your World (18%), #2 Deliver Returns (12%), #1 Tackle Big Issues (10%).

**Highest career success powers** (in order): #5 Walk the Halls (13%), #12 Aim Higher (13%), #6 You Go First (12%), #11 Know How You Inspire (12%).

**Quick diagnostic questions**:
1. Does your boss see you as revenue or cost?
2. Can you list the 3 biggest issues on your CEO's agenda right now?
3. Who outside marketing has meaningfully changed their behavior because of something you did in the last 6 months?
4. What's the biggest skill gap on your team right now?
5. Do your direct reports know exactly what they're accountable for and how they're being measured?
6. When did you last spend unstructured time with a customer?
7. What's your 5-year marketing leadership vision?

Weak answers point directly to which Powers need work.

## Key Concepts Reference

**V-Zone**: The overlap between customer needs and company needs. Expanding it is the primary goal of marketing leadership.

**Three gaps**: Trust gap (marketing is about the future), power gap (no direct authority over the customer experience), skills gap (technology changes faster than marketers can track).

**Eros-Logos spectrum**: Marketers tend toward Eros (creative, emotional, outward-looking). Most organizational leaders run on Logos (process, facts, numbers). Effective marketing leaders learn to bridge the two.

**Tribe**: A close-knit team with high trust and confidence, where members back each other, surface problems early, and take initiative without permission.

**Marketing leadership vs. marketing skills**: Technical marketing skills (brand strategy, campaign management, analytics) are the entry ticket. Leadership skills (the 12 Powers) are what determine long-term business impact and career success.
