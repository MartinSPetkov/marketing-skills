# Release Scale Template

From Chapter 12 of *Loved*. A shared scale that tells product, marketing, and sales what kind of thing is about to ship and therefore what kind of GTM lift it needs.

## Why a scale at all

Sprint, release, and launch are not synonyms. Teams use them interchangeably and end up confused about what's going public, when, and with how much noise.

**Sprint:** a unit of engineering work.
**Release:** code shipping to customers.
**Launch:** a coordinated GTM moment. Not every release is a launch.

The scale lets everyone agree on level first. Level 1 and Level 2 don't need launches. Level 4 and Level 5 do. The argument about "is this a launch" becomes a 30-second conversation about what level it is.

## The scale

| Level | Customer impact | Marketing objective | Resources / promotional vehicles | Lead time |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Level 1** | Invisible or near-invisible to the customer. Back-end upgrade. Internal tool. Tiny bug fix. | None required. | Release notes only. Docs team owns it. | Same week. |
| **Level 2** | Noticeable to power users but not to buyers. Minor UI change. Small feature. API tweak. | Awareness among existing customers. Retention play, not acquisition. | Release notes. In-app message. A line in the next monthly newsletter. | 1-2 weeks. |
| **Level 3** | Real feature. Existing customers care. A buyer evaluating you would want to know. | Awareness and reinforcement. Support sales conversations and upgrade paths. | Release notes. Blog post. Social. Sales enablement (one-pager, demo script). In-app. Email. | 4-6 weeks. |
| **Level 4** | Material product expansion. New module, new capability, new pricing tier. Buyers would shortlist you differently. | Acquisition and positioning shift. This changes how prospects see you. | Full press cycle (analyst briefings, tier-1 press, owned blog, customer webinar). Launch landing page. Sales playbook. Field enablement. Paid media. Customer comms. | 8-12 weeks. |
| **Level 5** | Company-defining. A new category, a re-platform, a pivot. Everything about how the company is understood shifts. | Category repositioning. Every channel carries the story. | Executive keynote. Large-scale press strategy. Analyst relations. Customer anchors. Event presence. Multi-quarter campaign. Internal all-hands. Sales retrain. | One quarter minimum. Often two. |

## The six-step build for your company

1. **Anchor levels to real past releases.** Not hypotheticals. "Level 3 looks like the X release from March 2025." This keeps calibration from drifting.
2. **Walk the scale with product, marketing, and sales together.** Each function needs to recognize the labels. A scale only product uses is worthless.
3. **Agree on the lead times.** When does PMM need to know? When does sales enablement need to start? Put numbers on it.
4. **Define who decides the level.** Usually a PMM and PM pair, escalating to VPs for Level 4 and Level 5.
5. **Put the scale on a shared page.** Confluence, Notion, a wiki. Not a slide. Somewhere everyone can find it.
6. **Review quarterly.** Calibration drifts. A Level 3 from last year may only feel like Level 2 now. Recalibrate against the current anchor releases.

## Common failure modes

**Scale used only by product.** Sales and marketing don't know the levels. Predictable result: sales argues every release is Level 5 because they want attention; marketing pushes back because they can't staff that; product feels ignored. Fix: train sales and marketing on the scale. Make it a shared document.

**Levels defined without customer-impact language.** "Level 3 is a medium release." That means nothing. Fix: anchor every level to what a customer would or would not notice.

**Hypothetical calibration.** "Level 4 is a big release." Big how? Fix: anchor each level to a real past release the team remembers.

**No lead time agreed.** Sales asks for Level 4 assets three days before ship. PMM panics. Nothing ships well. Fix: lead times are part of the scale, not a separate negotiation.

**Level inflation.** Every release becomes Level 3. Fix: recalibrate quarterly against anchor releases. Force a distribution (most releases are Level 1-2).

## How to calibrate a new release

Ask four questions:

1. Would a buyer evaluating us shortlist us differently because of this? If yes, Level 4 minimum.
2. Would our top customers fly in for a keynote about this? If yes, Level 5.
3. Would an existing customer be confused if they didn't see it mentioned anywhere? If yes, at least Level 3.
4. Does it change any contractual term, price, or entitlement? If yes, at least Level 3.

If none of the above: Level 1 or 2.

## Why the work matters

A Level 2 release marketed as Level 4 looks like overclaiming. A Level 4 release shipped as Level 2 wastes the opportunity. The scale is how you stop doing either.
