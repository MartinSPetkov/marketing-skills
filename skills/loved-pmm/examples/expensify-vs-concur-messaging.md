# Expensify vs Concur: two messaging strategies, same category

A worked example from Chapter 5 of *Loved*. Expense management software. Two companies. Two messaging strategies. Different results.

## Concur's messaging

Concur was (and remains) the enterprise incumbent. SAP acquired them in 2014. Their messaging, read from any Concur home page of that era:

> Concur takes your company completely out of the reactive mode by offering a cloud-based expense management solution that transforms spending management.

Read that aloud. It's dense. "Completely out of the reactive mode" is abstract. "Transforms spending management" could describe any software in the category. Nothing tells a buyer what the product actually does, who it's for, or why it's different from a competitor.

Concur got away with this for years because their sales motion was top-down enterprise. They won procurement battles, not messaging battles. But the messaging itself was brittle. It didn't help anyone sell, buy, or remember the product.

## Expensify's messaging

Expensify came at the same category from the bottom up. Their home page read:

> Expense reports that don't suck.

Six words. Customer-POV. Uses the words employees actually use about expense reports (they suck). Specific. Testable. Differentiated. Memorable.

## Why this matters

Lauchengco's point in Ch 5 is not that one company is better than the other. Both Concur and Expensify built successful businesses. The point is that messaging is a lever. You can pull it or let it go slack.

Concur's messaging was slack. It could have described any competitor in the category. It made no claim that a buyer could test. It did no work.

Expensify's messaging did work. It told a buyer: we know what expense reports feel like, we think they're terrible, our product is different. It gave sales reps a line they could actually say to a prospect. It gave marketing a theme to campaign on. It gave product a design principle (don't suck).

## The underlying PMM move: listen, then use the customer's words

Expensify's line didn't come from a whiteboard brainstorm. It came from listening to customers describe the category.

Lauchengco's listening questions from `references/messaging-process.md`:

> - What was hard or painful?
> - What surprised you?
> - What words do you use to describe it?

If you ask those questions about expense reports, the word "suck" comes up in the top five answers. Expensify's PMM heard it and wrote it down. Concur's PMM (if they had one) seemed to have filtered it out.

## CAST filter applied

Try running both lines through the CAST filter (Clear, Authentic, Simple, Testable).

**Expensify:**
- Clear: yes. Every word is concrete.
- Authentic: yes. Sounds like a person said it.
- Simple: yes. Six words.
- Testable: yes. A buyer can tell this message apart from Concur's.

**Concur:**
- Clear: no. "Out of the reactive mode" is abstract.
- Authentic: no. No human says this out loud.
- Simple: no. Four stacked claims.
- Testable: no. Swap Concur's name for Coupa's; the sentence still reads true.

## The business impact

Expensify grew rapidly in the SMB segment through the 2010s. The messaging was one reason (not the only one). The product genuinely was simpler and more delightful, but the messaging let that product reach the right customers and let the right customers describe the product to each other.

"Expense reports that don't suck" got said at dinner parties. "Cloud-based expense management solution that transforms spending management" did not.

## What this example does not mean

It doesn't mean all messaging should be six words. It doesn't mean profanity or near-profanity works for every brand. It doesn't mean enterprise buyers don't want substance.

It means: messaging does work or doesn't. Concur's messaging wasn't pulling weight. Expensify's was. When PMM writes home-page copy, the question to ask is: is this doing work, or is it decoration?

## How to use this example

When a user shares their own home-page headline or tagline for review, read it twice. Ask:

1. Could a competitor use this same line? If yes, it's not differentiated.
2. What word is the customer using that you're not? Find it. Use it.
3. Would a sales rep actually say this line in a meeting? If not, rewrite.
4. If this went on a billboard, would a person remember it tomorrow?

Push the user to get to something that passes all four. Most first drafts fail two or three.
