# iPhone and the adoption lifecycle

A worked example from Chapter 15 of *Loved*. The iPhone is the clearest case study of a product moving across the entire Technology Adoption Life Cycle, with messaging and GTM that changed as the customer changed.

## The adoption curve, quickly

Innovators → early adopters → early majority → late majority → laggards. Each segment buys for different reasons, responds to different proof, and tolerates different amounts of risk. Lauchengco's Ch 15 argues that most PMM mistakes come from applying the wrong-segment playbook to the wrong-segment buyer.

## iPhone Year 1 (2007): innovators and early adopters

The first iPhone was expensive, AT&T-locked, had no app store, no copy/paste, and a 2-megapixel camera. Innovators bought it anyway because it was a new kind of thing. The browser was a real browser. Multi-touch worked. You could watch YouTube on a phone.

Apple's messaging in 2007 led with capabilities that sounded like miracles:
- "Three products in one: iPod, phone, internet communicator"
- "The internet in your pocket"
- "It just works"

Pain-led and capability-led. Not feature specs. The innovator customer didn't need a comparison chart against a BlackBerry. They needed to believe the device could do something new.

## iPhone 3G and 3GS (2008-2009): early majority forms

Two changes made the early majority possible:

1. The App Store (2008). The iPhone went from a single-vendor device to a platform.
2. Enterprise email and exchange support (2009). The BlackBerry replacement story became credible.

Apple's messaging shifted. Less about "the internet in your pocket" (that was now a commodity claim as Android phones arrived). More about "there's an app for that." Category still forming, but differentiation layered on top.

Pricing moved too: lower entry price, carrier subsidy. Pricing and messaging are levers that work together. See `references/pricing-lever.md`.

## iPhone 4 through 6 (2010-2014): late majority, mainstream sale

By the iPhone 4 (2010), the category was settled. Phones had apps. Phones were pocket computers. The question was not "should I have a smartphone" but "which one."

Apple's messaging shifted to features that showed craftsmanship: Retina display, aluminum body, FaceTime, Siri. Quality and polish cues. Late-majority buyers don't tolerate bugs or awkwardness. They want the safe choice.

This is Lauchengco's rule from Ch 15:

> The chasm between early adopters and early majority isn't technical. It's psychological. Early adopters buy possibility. Early majority buys pragmatism.

Every release cycle, Apple added reliability features and incremental improvements. "Best camera ever." "Fastest iPhone ever." This sounds lazy unless you understand that reliability and incremental improvement are exactly what the late majority wants to hear.

## iPhone 12 through iPhone 16 Pro (2020-present): laggards pulled in

Late-stage Apple messaging is aspirational and identity-led. "Shot on iPhone" galleries. Cinema-grade video. Privacy as a brand promise. Ecosystem lock-in (AirPods, Watch, Mac, home). The iPhone is no longer a phone. It's a piece of identity and a hub.

Laggards (including people who hold 4-year-old phones) are still replacing devices. They buy because the old one breaks, because their grandkids FaceTime, because the bank app requires a newer OS. Messaging to laggards doesn't try to sell aspiration. It sells "upgrade now, your pictures will be better, your calls will be clearer."

Apple runs multiple messaging tracks simultaneously for this reason. Pro customers see cinematography. Late-majority customers see "everything easier." Laggards see "your old phone is slowing you down."

## What the PMM learns

### One product, five segments, five messaging strategies

A PMM with one messaging deck for all five segments will underserve four of them. Segment messaging is not an optional polish layer. It's structural.

### Don't cross the chasm with innovator messaging

The most expensive mistake Lauchengco describes is a startup with strong innovator love trying to scale to the early majority with innovator messaging. "Be the first to..." / "Revolutionary new..." / "For visionaries." The early majority doesn't want to be a visionary. They want a product that works and has references from companies like theirs.

The messaging shift has to happen before the customer shift does. If you wait until your growth stalls, you're already late.

### The Superhuman counterpoint (Ch 15)

Superhuman ran intentional scarcity. Waitlist. Hand-holding onboarding. $30/month. This looked premium, but Lauchengco's read is that Superhuman was ruthlessly targeting only innovators and early adopters. They were not trying to cross the chasm. They were trying to dominate the left tail first.

Eventually Superhuman has to decide if it crosses the chasm. The choice has consequences. Crossing means simpler pricing, less hand-holding, different messaging. Not crossing means living off a small, wealthy audience.

### Segment warning: "We're for everyone"

When a founder or PMM says the target customer is everyone, the actual result is that no segment gets served well. Apple didn't target everyone. Apple targeted innovators first, early adopters next, early majority after that. The sequencing was deliberate. The "for everyone" messaging came only after the sequencing worked.

## How to use this example

When advising a user on segment and messaging, walk through the iPhone arc as an anchor. Then ask:

1. Which segment are you actually selling to this quarter?
2. What's the messaging leading with (pain, differentiation, aspiration)?
3. Are those two in the same stage?

If they aren't, the messaging is fighting the segment. Fix the message to the segment, or change the segment to match the message. You can't keep both misaligned and hit growth targets.
