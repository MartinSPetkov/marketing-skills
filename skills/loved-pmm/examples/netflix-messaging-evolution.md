# Netflix messaging evolution

A worked example drawn from Chapter 16 of *Loved*. How a single brand moved its messaging across three distinct stages as the product category shifted underneath it.

## The three eras

**Era 1: DVD by mail (1997-2007).** Category: video rental. Competitor: Blockbuster. Messaging: "No late fees. No trips to the store." Problem-led. Specific. Anchored to a pain customers felt weekly.

**Era 2: Streaming pioneer (2007-2013).** Category: streaming. Competitors: cable, iTunes rentals. Messaging: "Watch TV shows and movies instantly." Differentiation-led. The category was new to most customers; Netflix had to teach the behavior first, then anchor why Netflix was the place to do it.

**Era 3: Original content studio (2013+).** Category: entertainment, full stop. Competitors: HBO, Disney, networks. Messaging evolved to center on shows: "Stranger Things. Narcos. Bridgerton." Aspirational identity. Netflix is where the cultural moments happen.

## What each stage teaches

### Stage 1: lead with the problem

DVD-by-mail messaging didn't try to be aspirational. It didn't say "rediscover your love of movies." It said you don't have to pay late fees and you don't have to drive anywhere. A buyer could say yes or no to that pitch in five seconds.

Lauchengco's rule from Ch 24:

> The earlier in a product's life you are, the more you must tell a story using the pain and problems that make you necessary.

Netflix Era 1 is textbook. Pain-led. Specific. Low altitude.

### Stage 2: differentiate within an emerging category

By 2007 the problem was changing. Customers were starting to understand streaming as a possibility (iTunes rentals had primed the pump). Netflix's job wasn't to argue that digital distribution was better than DVDs. It was to own the category as it formed.

Messaging shifted from "no late fees" to "watch instantly, anywhere." The differentiation wasn't Netflix vs Blockbuster anymore. It was Netflix vs "you don't have streaming yet." Category expansion messaging.

This is Ch 24's middle case: the customer knows they need a solution; the question is why yours. Netflix said: because it's already here, it works on your TV, and it's $8.

### Stage 3: earn aspiration

Only after a decade of streaming leadership did Netflix's messaging get aspirational. "See what's next." "Netflix is a joy." Ads that sell the feeling of weekend binges. Shows that become cultural events.

Lauchengco:

> Late-stage, category-leading products can lead with aspiration. Nike doesn't sell shoes; it sells athletic identity.

Netflix earned that altitude with two decades of problem-led and differentiation-led work. An early-stage startup cannot skip stages and talk like Netflix in 2026. It reads as pretension.

## The PMM lesson

Same brand, three different messaging strategies across the life of the product. A PMM at Netflix in 2001 who tried to pitch "see what's next" would have been laughed out of the room. A PMM at Netflix in 2022 who tried to pitch "no late fees" would look like they'd time-traveled from the wrong decade.

Right message, right time. The job of PMM is to know which stage the product is in and what the customer actually needs to hear next. See `references/category-strategy.md` for the framework.

## What this means for your own messaging

Diagnose stage first. If your product is in Stage 1 (problem awareness is low, customers don't yet feel the pain urgently), your messaging is pain-led. If you're in Stage 2 (customers know the category but are choosing between alternatives), lead with differentiation. If you're in Stage 3 (category leader, earned position), aspiration works.

Most mistakes happen when a Stage 1 product tries to talk like Stage 3, or a Stage 3 product still talks like Stage 1 and bores the market.

## What Netflix didn't do

Netflix never pretended to be a category creator. Streaming existed in some form before Netflix (YouTube, iTunes, Joost). Netflix redefined the category by owning the catalog, the UX, the binge format, and the originals. Lauchengco's point in Ch 24: redefining beats creating almost every time. Netflix is on the right column of her Table 24.1 for this reason.

A founder who says "we're the Netflix of X" usually wants to be a category creator. The actual lesson from Netflix is the opposite: find the "you are here" marker customers already know, and redefine what winning in that category looks like.
