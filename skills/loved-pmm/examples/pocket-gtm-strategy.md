# Pocket: a GTM case study in strategic choices

A worked example drawn from Lauchengco's Pocket anchor in Chapters 2, 3, and 19 of *Loved*. Pocket is a save-for-later reading app that Lauchengco helped advise through a multi-year GTM refinement. It shows how the four fundamentals interact in practice.

## Where Pocket started

Pocket (originally "Read It Later") was a well-loved indie app with a loyal base of power users. Good engineering. Good product design. Thin GTM. The growth curve was flat. Revenue unclear.

Most apps in this position either die or get acquired. Pocket eventually got acquired by Mozilla (2017), but before that, the work Lauchengco describes was about getting the GTM to match the quality of the product.

## The four fundamentals in Pocket's case

See `references/four-fundamentals.md` for the framework.

### Ambassador: bringing the market back in

Pocket's team knew their power users well. Where they were weak: understanding why the product wasn't resonating with the broader market. Research showed that casual readers didn't even recognize the problem Pocket solved. They were saving links with bookmarks or browser tabs and didn't feel that acutely painful.

The fix was not to teach everyone to feel the pain. It was to focus on the segments where the pain was sharp: researchers, students, heavy content consumers, commuters on mobile. Narrowing the ICP (ideal customer profile) was the first win.

### Strategist: making hard tradeoffs

Pocket faced a choice most small companies face: breadth or depth. They could try to be a casual tool for everyone, or they could be an excellent tool for a specific use case.

Lauchengco's strategy advice pushed toward depth. Own "the most-loved reading app for people who read on the go." Don't try to be Evernote-light. Don't try to be a full knowledge-management tool. Stay a reading app that's great at reading.

This sounds obvious in retrospect. In the moment it meant killing features on the roadmap, saying no to potential partners, rejecting growth tactics that would bring casual users who wouldn't stick.

### Storyteller: fitting message to segment

The initial messaging leaned on productivity and organization framings. "Save articles for later." "Organize your reading list." It performed fine but didn't differentiate.

The shift: lean into the reading experience itself. Typography. The offline mode for commuters. Text-to-speech so you could listen on your run. The message moved from "storage" to "a better way to read."

This is right-message-right-time (see `references/category-strategy.md`). Reading apps as a category existed. Pocket was redefining what winning looked like in that category: the best experience, not the biggest storage.

### Evangelist: finding and activating the right fans

Pocket had evangelists. Writers who linked to Pocket's save button. Podcasters who read their show notes aloud from the app. Heavy users who ranked articles in year-end lists. The work was to recognize these people, not discover them.

The PMM move was to formalize the evangelist program: featured reader lists, curated recommendations, integration with Kobo and Kindle, partnerships with browsers. Each of these activated existing evangelists instead of trying to manufacture new ones.

## What the full PGTM looked like

Imagined as a Product Go-to-Market Canvas (see `references/product-gtm-canvas.md`):

**Customer row.** Narrow ICP (researchers, students, commuters, heavy content consumers). Track whether casual users are starting to feel the pain (they weren't, which is why ICP stayed narrow).

**Product row.** Reading experience upgrades. Offline mode. Text-to-speech. Highlight and tag. Not a general note-taking platform.

**Marketing row.** Content partnerships. Thought leadership in "reading on mobile" conversations. Integrations with publishers and hardware makers.

**Activities row.** Featured reader lists, browser extensions, mobile OS integrations. Small, concrete, owned.

## The acquisition

Mozilla acquired Pocket in 2017 for an undisclosed sum. The acquisition wasn't driven by revenue (Pocket was small) but by strategic fit. Mozilla wanted a content-discovery engine for Firefox. Pocket gave them curated reading with a real audience.

Lauchengco's read: a product that had tightened its positioning and deepened its segment appeal is far more attractive as an acquisition target than a product spread thin. The GTM work made Pocket acquirable.

## The PMM lesson

Most apps in Pocket's position try to broaden before they deepen. They add features. They chase adjacent use cases. They dilute the ICP. And they die or get acquired for less than they should.

Pocket's counter-move was to narrow, not broaden. Deeper ICP. Sharper message. Focused evangelism. Product roadmap in service of the reading experience, not the casual-user surface area.

## How to use this example

When a user describes a product that feels "loved by its users but not growing," walk through Pocket. Ask:

1. Is the ICP narrow enough? Or is it trying to be for everyone?
2. Is the message about the product's category, or about the product's best use case? (Different answers are appropriate at different stages.)
3. Who are the evangelists, and what are you doing to activate them?
4. What's on the roadmap that doesn't serve the sharpest use case? Can it be cut?

The usual answer is that the product needs to get smaller in scope before it can get bigger in reach. That's counterintuitive and it's where PMM earns its keep.
