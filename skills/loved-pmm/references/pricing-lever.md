# Pricing and Packaging

Chapter 17. Lauchengco's core claim: pricing is value engineering, not cost accounting. Her chapter is short, opinionated, and easy to misquote, so this reference sticks close to her frames.

## The Nike sunglasses anchor

Focus group at a San Francisco women's marathon. A $300 pair (initially the favorite) fell to last after price reveal. A $125 performance pair (initially fourth) became top pick.

Lauchengco's reading: "The perceived value matched my desired use case." She was a runner, not a fashion buyer. The $300 pair was priced for someone with a different use case entirely.

Lesson: pricing tests work best when you show the price in context of the use case. Absent price, people vote aesthetics. With price, they vote purpose.

## The core principle

> Pricing is not about what it costs to produce a service. It's about people's perceived value of a product and their willingness to pay. Pricing is value engineering.

Cost-plus pricing is fine for commodity goods. For software and subscriptions, cost structure is decoupled from value. A feature that costs pennies to run can be worth thousands because of what it enables. The job is to find where customer-perceived value is, and price to it.

## Three distinct concepts

People conflate these and then have the wrong meeting with the wrong people.

- **Monetization strategy.** How and when you make money. Joint: product, business operations, PMM.
- **Pricing strategy.** The actual price. Finance or business ops drives; sales has input.
- **Packaging strategy.** Bundles that serve customers, segments, or use cases. Typically PMM-driven. Product partners.

PMM's strongest ownership is packaging. PMM has heavy input on pricing. PMM contributes to monetization but doesn't own it alone.

If you're a PMM and someone asks you "what should this cost?" you should first ask which question they're actually asking. Monetization, pricing, or packaging are three different conversations with different participants.

## Four essentials for a pricing metric

The pricing metric is the unit you price against. Per-user, per-transaction, per-GB, per-API-call, flat.

1. **Match the metric to value.** The metric should grow as the product delivers more value. Dropbox priced per TB because storage value scales with volume. Per-user works for tools where headcount and value track. A mismatched metric invites abuse or stalling.

2. **Simple enough for head math.** Per-user is sticky because a team can calculate it by counting seats. Usage-based infrastructure pricing scares buyers because the bill is unpredictable. Consider pre-packaging at usage thresholds so customers can use more without overpayment fear.

3. **Easily measurable.** IT and compliance teams need to know their compliance status without auditing their own usage. Per-seat looks simple but can impede adoption, because each new user is a new ask.

4. **CFO or procurement-friendly.** Procurement compares total cost of ownership and ROI across contracts. Your differentiators don't show up in their spreadsheet. Your metric has to.

## Cheap vs. best

Two pricing psychologies exist: "those who buy because it's cheap and those who buy because it's the best." A product picks one end. The middle is where positioning dies.

iPhone's premium launch pricing was about keeping the beachhead exclusive. It was a brand move as much as a revenue move.

Lauchengco's price-ceiling test:

> If you don't hear customer complaints that you're expensive, you're leaving money on the table.

Anecdote from a startup sales leader: "I basically double the price at every meeting and am waiting for someone to blink. So far, that hasn't happened." That's a product with room to run on price.

## When to establish repeatable pricing

B2B: wait until 20 to 30 customers have gone through the pipeline before setting repeatable guardrails. Before that, you don't know willingness to pay across your real segment, so any pricing rule is guesswork dressed as policy.

B2C: revisit frequently. Online testing is cheap; run it.

## Packaging strategies (Table 17.1)

Three patterns, each fit for a stage.

| | All-inclusive | Platform + Add-ons | Good, Better, Best |
|---|---|---|---|
| **When to use** | Early product | Expanding product line(s) and feature sets | Well-defined use cases and market segments |
| **Why** | Simple for customers, simple for sales | Flexible for customers, brings complexity, lets sales customize | Drive highest possible ASP, less decision fatigue, repeatable sales execution |

A few rules:

- The entry-level edition should include must-haves. The pitch is "look at all I get for this price." If the entry level is a stripped artifact, buyers churn up to consideration of competitors.
- Next-level editions hold must-haves for specific segments.
- Additional editions or add-ons hold niche, highly specific use cases.

## Subscription shift

Microsoft Office Home & Student was $104.99 one-time. Microsoft 365 is $99.99 per year. Same family of products, different business model. Cloud and subscription change what customers compare to. If you're still pricing like perpetual software in a subscription market, you're training customers to see you as expensive.

## Pricing tensions with sales

Sales wants to win the deal in front of them. Pricing wants to protect the book across all deals. The job is managing the tension.

Sales input on pricing is essential. Sales ownership of pricing is not. "Sales-driven pricing" means every price is a floor and the next deal discounts from it. PMM's role is to hold guardrails while letting sales negotiate inside them.

## How to work with a pricing question

When a user brings a pricing problem, do this:

1. Clarify which conversation they're having. Monetization, pricing, or packaging.
2. Ask what the customer values that they're willing to pay for. Not what the product costs to build.
3. Ask who the buyer is and how they procure. The metric has to work for them.
4. Ask what segment or use case they're pricing for. Different segments often deserve different packages, not different prices of the same package.
5. Ask what the company's pricing psychology is. Cheap or best. Hybrid is rarely the right answer.
6. Ask when they last heard "you're too expensive" from a target buyer. If never, there's probably room.

Only then pick a package structure from the table above, and only then pick a number.

## What this reference doesn't cover
