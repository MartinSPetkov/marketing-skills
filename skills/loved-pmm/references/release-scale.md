# The Release Scale

The simplest and most useful tool in the book. Chapter 12. Solves the agile-era problem of releases flying through engineering faster than GTM can react, with engineers feeling their work goes unmarketed and PMMs getting surprised by launches.

## What problem the scale solves

Three terms get collapsed in most teams. Separate them first:

- **Sprint.** Short time-boxed period for a set amount of work. May not mean public access.
- **Release.** Makes public a new product or combination of features. Usually involves coordinated GTM action.
- **Launch.** Major, cross-functionally-supported release the whole company gets behind, usually date-anchored. "Most companies won't attempt more than one to two major launches a year."

Everything else in agile-age chaos comes from teams sliding between these terms without realizing.

## What the Release Scale is

A shared taxonomy between product and GTM teams. Levels (or grades, tiers, numbers, names) that rank releases by market impact and the GTM investment that comes with them. Once the team has a scale and has calibrated it against real past releases, they can plan with sentences like "this is a Level 3 release" and everyone means the same thing.

Level 1 is minimum GTM investment. Each successive level adds to the previous one, so a Level 5 is Level 4 plus Level 5-specific activity. Typical scales have three to five levels; pick what you need.

## How to build one

Seven steps:

1. **Decide on the calibration scale.** Levels, grades, names, numbers, or tiers. Use something people intuit without a legend. If you need a glossary to read the scale, simplify.
2. **Use known past releases as examples to define the levels.** Not hypothetical future ones. Ask: "What does this level look like? Oh, like the X release from last March." Concrete examples stabilize meaning.
3. **Identify customer impact per level.** Low, medium, high. Sometimes with finer gradations for the top levels.
4. **Define marketing objectives per level.** Why does a release at this level exist from a market perspective? Not "to build awareness." Specific: "educate existing customers about a capability they already wanted," or "open a new buyer persona," or "anchor analyst coverage for the year."
5. **Define typical resources and promotional vehicles per level.** Level 1 might be: release notes, knowledge-base update, support team enablement. Level 5 might be: press tour, analyst briefings, landing page, customer webinar, partner announcements, executive blog, video series. Each level inherits from the ones below.
6. **Articulate lead times.** Website updates, legal reviews, copy editing, design, press pitching, and customer support readiness can take weeks. If a release needs a video, a webinar, partner quotes, and regulatory review, six weeks is a floor, not a ceiling.
7. **Talk about releases using the scale in planning meetings.** "From the product team's perspective, is this a Level 2 or Level 3?" If there's disagreement, surface it. Remind teams that one to two Level 5 releases a year are important: "companies that lack big visible movements forward start to give the perception they've stagnated."

## An example scale (illustrative, not prescriptive)

Scales are company-specific. This gives shape to what a filled scale looks like.

**Level 1 — Maintenance.** Bug fixes, minor UI polish. Customer impact: low. Marketing objective: none, beyond accuracy of release notes. Resources: release notes, support enablement. Lead time: 1 day.

**Level 2 — Feature improvement.** A meaningful enhancement to an existing feature. Customer impact: low to medium for the subset using that feature. Marketing objective: inform existing users. Resources: in-app announcement, knowledge-base article, changelog blog. Lead time: 1 to 2 weeks.

**Level 3 — Net-new feature.** A feature customers will look for. Customer impact: medium. Marketing objective: drive adoption among existing customers; give sales a new story for active deals. Resources: Level 2 plus blog post, landing page update, sales enablement email, webinar. Lead time: 3 to 4 weeks.

**Level 4 — Major capability.** A capability that opens a new use case or segment. Customer impact: high. Marketing objective: reposition the product around the new capability. Resources: Level 3 plus press outreach, analyst briefing, customer video, paid campaign. Lead time: 6 to 8 weeks.

**Level 5 — Flagship launch.** Category-shaping release. Customer impact: high. Marketing objective: own a new narrative for the year; refresh positioning. Resources: Level 4 plus press tour, launch event, executive thought leadership, partner announcements, multi-week content series. Lead time: 8 to 12 weeks, sometimes longer.

## How to use it once built

The scale is not a spec. It's a forecasting and negotiation tool.

- **In roadmap planning.** Every release under discussion gets tagged. "If this is a Level 4, we need to start GTM six weeks out. Can we move ship date?" or "If we can ship this as a Level 2, we can get it out in a sprint."
- **In status updates.** "Level 2 ships Tuesday. Level 4 is the mid-quarter focus. Level 5 is Q4."
- **In conflict resolution.** When sales argues a feature should get more noise, the scale forces specificity. What level? What resources? What lead time?
- **In leadership reporting.** Leadership can scan a quarter and see the shape of market output without reading feature-level specs.

## Agile marketing principles that come with the scale (Ch 12)

The scale lives inside an agile marketing operating model. Lauchengco's six principles:

- Respond to changes versus following a plan.
- Rapid iterations over big-bang campaigns.
- Testing and data over opinions and conventions.
- Numerous small experiments over a few large bets.
- Individuals and interactions over "large" market segments.
- Collaboration over silos and hierarchy.

### The highest-functioning version

"PMMs act like PMs with a marketing squad, leading weekly scrums with specialists (communications, advertising, digital, web, design). This is a 'learnings in' not 'reporting out' type meeting."

The scrum is not status. It's learning. Specialists bring back what they saw in the market the past week. PMM routes learning into the backlog and into GTM decisions. If your weekly marketing meeting is a status read-out, it's not agile marketing.

## Common failure modes

**A scale built with hypothetical releases.** Calibration drifts within two quarters. Always anchor to real past releases.

**A scale that only product uses.** Sales and marketing don't know the levels. Predictable result: level inflation, because sales loudly argues every release is a Level 5.

**A five-level scale used as a ceiling.** Teams start producing mostly Level 2 and 3 releases because Level 5 requires too much. The scale should force the quarterly conversation "where are our big moves?"

**A scale without lead times.** Everyone agrees Level 4 needs more marketing, nobody agrees when that marketing has to start. Ships late and half-supported.
