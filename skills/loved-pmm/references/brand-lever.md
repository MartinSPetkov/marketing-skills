# Brand as a PMM Lever

Chapter 16. Brand is usually treated as someone else's job (the head of brand, the creative director, the agency). Lauchengco reframes it as a lever PMM pulls at specific moments, most often around product naming, suite architecture, and segment shifts.

## What brand is, in this book's definition

> Brand is more about a consistent experience across every aspect of how a company acts. It's a promise between a customer and a company.

Not the logo. Not the Super Bowl ad. The accumulation of every experience a customer has, weighed against the expectation the company created.

## The Netflix anchor

Netflix brand was built by a consistently great experience: red envelopes arriving quickly, easy anywhere-watching. Not a marketing campaign. The story Gibson Biddle tells: the product team decided to proactively notify customers before charging their credit card at the end of a trial, knowing it would lose tens of millions of revenue per month. Brand chosen over revenue. "That decision was tens of millions of global subscribers ago."

Lesson: the moments brand gets built are often product decisions that look like revenue giveaways. PMM's role is to flag those moments and argue for the brand side.

## Three brand levels

1. **Company.** Apple, Microsoft, Salesforce.
2. **Line of business or key brand.** Music, TV, Watch, iPhone, Mac.
3. **Product.** MacBook Air, MacBook Pro, iMac.

Not every level gets marketed. Apple markets at product level. Microsoft tends to market at line-of-business or suite level (Office, Xbox, Azure). The question for any PMM is: which level does the customer encounter first, and which level should carry the story?

## Four brand situations where PMM gets pulled in

Lauchengco names these specifically because they recur in her consulting work.

**Product scope expands and market perception is narrow.**
Pocket's rebrand from Read It Later. The product did more than save articles, but the old name fenced the story in. The rename also opened the door to Pocket Hits, the weekly email. One brand change unlocked a content channel.

**Product-brand loyalty helps acquire new audiences.**
Intuit's QuickBooks and TurboTax are better known than the Intuit company brand. Atlassian's Jira is dominant among developers, and Jira Service Management and Jira Align inherited credibility from the Jira name. In these cases, the product name does the heavy lifting and the company brand sits behind.

**Penetrate a new market with a new brand.**
Microsoft went to Xbox because the enterprise brand didn't fit gaming. Going in as "Microsoft" would have pre-lost the segment.

**Unify under a product-suite name.**
When multiple products target the same audience, a suite name simplifies GTM. Customers stop choosing which product; they choose the suite and grow inside it.

## Product naming is brand strategy

Naming is often treated as a creative exercise. It isn't. It's a positioning decision that locks in whether products feel related, where they sit relative to competitors, and what story the portfolio tells.

Salesforce Marketing Cloud uses "Studio" as a suffix: Email Studio, Audience Studio, Mobile Studio, Social Studio, Data Studio. Consistent suffixes announce family membership without requiring a visual cue. Contrast with a portfolio of names that share nothing; the customer has to do the memory work.

When a user asks you about a product name, don't optimize for "catchy." Ask how this name sits with the other names in the portfolio, what it tells a buyer about the product's place, and whether it scales if more products join the family.

## The Netscape example, larger battlefront

At Netscape, "as goes the browser, so goes Netscape." A company brand problem played out as a product war with Microsoft's Internet Explorer. The sale to AOL happened because the Netscape brand captured a segment AOL couldn't reach. Brand as asset in an acquisition, not just a marketing deliverable.

Lesson: when you can't win a product war, the brand may still be worth something. And when you're in a product war, remember the brand stakes, not just the feature stakes.

## Brand work is slow, and PMM has to defend the slowness

Brand is the longest-feedback loop in marketing. A campaign spike is visible in a week. A brand shift takes a year or more to measure. PMM leadership often has to defend brand investment against demand-gen peers whose metrics move faster.

The defense: brand investment reduces CAC over time. Search behavior, word of mouth, direct traffic, sales cycle time, and competitive win rate all move when brand moves. If the scorecard only tracks cost-per-lead, brand work will always lose the argument. Fight for scorecard inclusion before the budget debate, not during it.

## When to pull the brand lever

A few triggers where the right PMM move is to treat the question as a brand question, not just a campaign question:

- Sales starts hearing "I've never heard of you" at the top of every call.
- Customers don't know you have a second product.
- Your product and your company names are working against each other (one known, one unknown).
- An acquisition or rebrand by a competitor has redefined the category and your name sounds dated.
- You're trying to reach a new segment and the current brand doesn't fit that segment's world.

In each case, the answer isn't a bigger ad budget. It's a brand-level decision about naming, architecture, or positioning. That decision is PMM's to propose and the CMO's to own.
