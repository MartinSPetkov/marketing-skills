# The Messaging Canvas

Chapter 25. Lauchengco's second canvas. Where the PGTM Canvas aligns a year of activity, the Messaging Canvas aligns what the company actually says about a specific product or product family.

Use with `references/messaging-process.md` for the discovery technique that feeds this canvas.

## The anchor story

A company losing head-to-head deals. A senior leadership meeting called to fix it. Each leader stuffed their own ideas into the pitch. The PMM, an individual contributor, rolled them in. No testing. Result: tolerable but not good.

Lauchengco's reading:

> Coming up with great messaging and the story to embed it in is a team sport, but it is product marketing's job to collect the inputs and craft a strong output.

The Messaging Canvas exists because this scene repeats in every company. Without a constraint on the page, every leader adds their pet phrase and the output dies of too many hands.

## Purpose of the canvas

Separate messaging into its building blocks so you can use the right pieces at the right time. The blocks are positioning, customer benefits, the how (what the product actually does), and proof (why anyone should believe the claims).

Force-constrain it to one page. The constraint is the point. If it goes past one page, you haven't prioritized.

## The canvas fields (Figure 25.1)

In order, top to bottom.

1. **Customer segments.** The most important audiences. Use a unique symbol per segment (square, circle, triangle) to tag messages below. Not every audience, just the priority ones.

2. **Positioning statement / starter messaging.** A short version of the product's position: what it does, what makes it different. This is the working draft, not the published slogan.

3. **Key support messages / pillars.** Two to four pillars that make the company credible and intriguing. Typical pillar shapes: a broad benefit, a novel way of doing something, the ecosystem you're in, the technologies unlocked. Pillars are the frame that holds everything else up.

4. **Areas of value in customer-friendly language.** Under each pillar, the specific benefits or use cases customers care about. Tagged by segment symbol. Not feature lists; features tied to benefits or use cases.

5. **Evidence.** Customer stories, use cases, data, research or analyst quotes, proven ROI. The evidence section should feel "full of facts" and credible.

Lauchengco:

> Messaging is just as much about what others say about your product as what you say about it.

The evidence row is where the others' voices live.

## The seven-step build

**Setup: messaging discovery.** Open-ended questions plus micro-surveys. How would customers describe this to a friend? What problems make them look for a better way? "Why didn't you try our service?" "What can we do better to meet your needs?" See `references/messaging-process.md` for the full question set.

**Step 1: Decide on the most important segments.** Don't pick all audiences. Pick the ones that matter most this year.

**Step 2: Starter messaging and key support pillars.** Draft the short positioning statement. Identify the two to four pillars.

**Step 3: Areas of value in customer-friendly language.** Under each pillar, list the specific value. Tie features to benefits or use cases. Use words a customer would use.

**Step 4: Designate messages as appropriate for specific audiences.** Use the segment symbols from the top row. The same message can apply to multiple segments. Some messages are segment-specific.

**Step 5: Provide evidence.** Stories are easier to remember and share than data. Include both. Keep a running list; rotate in and out.

**Step 6: Test with customers in intended media.** Email, website, presentation. If you have a sales force, start with sales. Lauchengco:

> You're not looking for customers saying "This is great!" Rather, you're looking for anything that drives curiosity and deepens conversation.

Look for emotional chords (a phrase that makes them lean in), third rails (something that kills the conversation), and yellow-brick roads (a line that leads them into more questions).

**Step 7: Refine.** Remember Netflix's "Cancel Anytime" wasn't about the product; it was about trust. What works might not be about your product. Only include a tagline if you want everyone using it. Practice the messaging out loud. Listen for jargon and forced phrasing. Run a final CAST check.

## FAQ

**Timing.** Startups: first pass in less than a week; test and refine for a month; update frequently. Mature companies: test and refine can take months; then messaging stays stable for the life cycle of a major release.

**How much customer input.**

> A lot, but messaging is customer informed, not customer driven. Customer insights are inputs. Only you have all the pieces around them, plus market, technology, and business needs.

**Multi-product companies.** One canvas per product or product family. Don't cram everything onto one page.

**If it goes past one page.** You haven't prioritized. Cut until it fits.

## The IndexTank example

Search-as-a-service. Through messaging discovery, IndexTank found a third segment they hadn't planned for: developer evangelists who wouldn't use the product themselves but tested new tech and engaged on developer forums. After testing on the website, the team refined to "Easily add powerful search to your site. Good search is good for business." Why that line? Because curious developer evangelists weren't paying customers; larger websites were. The positioning shifted to what their most important customers needed to hear.

Lesson: the canvas will reveal segments you didn't plan for. Give them a symbol, tag the messages, and then decide whether they're the priority or not. Don't silently prioritize your way out of hearing them.

## How to co-build this with a user

When a user brings a messaging problem, offer to build the canvas together. Start from their context (product, audience, current copy). Use `assets/messaging-canvas-template.md` as the blank.

Order of operations:

1. Ask them who the priority segments are. Push back if they say "everyone."
2. Ask them what phrases customers actually use. If they don't know, pause the canvas and run discovery first.
3. Draft starter messaging from customer language, not marketing language.
4. Pick two to four pillars. If they want more, the canvas is too big.
5. Fill in value per pillar using customer-friendly phrases.
6. Name the evidence you actually have. If the evidence slot is thin, the whole canvas has a credibility problem and you need to go fix that before shipping messaging.

Don't produce a filled canvas in under ten minutes. The value is in the conversation, not the artifact.

## Common failure modes

**Pillars that are just feature groups.** "Fast, Secure, Reliable" is not pillars; it's an adjective shelf. A real pillar has a reason for being there that ties to a customer perception you're trying to win.

**Customer-friendly language that is actually feature language in fewer words.** "Advanced analytics" is still jargon; it's shorter jargon. Test the phrases with a customer before trusting them.

**Evidence full of company announcements.** Funding rounds and hires aren't evidence for a buyer. Customer stories, third-party quotes, and outcome data are.

**A tagline in the canvas.** Lauchengco's rule: "Only include a tagline if you want everyone using it." A tagline on the canvas signals that the entire company should repeat it. Most canvases shouldn't have one.

**Filled in by one person in one afternoon.** The canvas needs the room. The room is where the disagreements surface and get resolved.
