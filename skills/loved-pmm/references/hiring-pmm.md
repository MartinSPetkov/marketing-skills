# Hiring PMM

Chapter 27. Lauchengco's most concrete chapter on hiring, with a signature interview question and a skills rubric that actually works.

## The three-candidate anchor

An early-stage startup had three finalists.

- **The curious.** Adapted in real-time. Admitted what she didn't know. Genuine product curiosity.
- **The communicator.** Polished. Crisp talk track. Interactive in the prepared parts. Weak off-script.
- **The collaborator.** Navigated organizations well. Used management frameworks. Track record of building relationships.

Lauchengco recommended the curious one.

> Adaptability and curiosity were the more important skills for their hire.

The lesson is stage-specific. At an early-stage company where the market isn't figured out, adaptability beats polish. At a mature company with stable segments, the collaborator might win. Don't apply the same rubric to every stage.

## The skills rubric

Six dimensions. Each has an interview signal and an interview question.

### Deep customer curiosity and active listening

**Evidence to look for.** Ability to adapt a point of view. Samples of customer-centric work.

**Interview question.** "Can you talk through a time when you had a plan but changed it based on something new you learned?"

**What to probe.** Push back on their assumptions during the answer. Watch whether they reconsider or dig in.

### Product curiosity and technical competence

**Evidence to look for.** Unprompted product talk. Moves across different jobs or industries. Sincere interest in a category or product.

**Warning.**

> Beware of being seduced by industry expertise. It can be a double-edged sword. Great knowledge of a space can mean a fixed mindset.

**Interview question.** "What were you looking for when you jumped from [X] to [Y]? What lessons did you apply from that job into how you did your next job? What product do you love right now?"

### Strategy, execution, and business savvy

**Evidence to look for.** Results tied to business outcomes. Collaborative leverage of teams. Ability to map launches back to business impact.

**Interview question.** "What do you consider your biggest success so far? How did you know it was a success?"

If they can't name a concrete measure of success, that's a flag. PMMs who describe success only as "the launch went well" have never been held to a business outcome.

### Collaborative

**Evidence to look for.** Systems and processes, not just relationships.

**Interview question.** "What is the best product/sales/product-marketing relationship you've seen in action? What made it good?"

Good candidates describe specific mechanics: standing meetings, shared documents, escalation protocols. Weaker candidates describe the people being friendly.

### Written and verbal communication

**How to assess.** Use exercises. Watch for candidates who make presentations interactive rather than one-way.

> The very best presenters will not just be engaging; they will make the presentation interactive.

Probe off-script. Interrupt. Change the question. See whether the polish survives improvisation.

### Broad marketing knowledge

Not expert at everything. Enough to guide PGTM activities. Not a specialist in digital, a specialist in content, a specialist in events. Someone who knows how each of those plays a role and when to pull which lever.

## The signature question

Lauchengco's one interview question that beats every other question in her experience.

> Tell me about a product or company you think is doing really great marketing. It can be anything. And tell me why you think it's great.

Corollary, after their answer:

> Now pretend you're a marketing leader at a competitor to the great-marketing company. What two or three things would you consider most important to do in order to compete with [company] well?

Why this question works:

> Neither of you has an information advantage. It forces you to pay attention to their thinking at least as much as their answers.

Ten minutes. That's the whole interview block. You learn more about how a PMM thinks from this question than from an hour of STAR-format behavioral interviewing.

## What to listen for during the signature question

**Breadth of marketing tool set.** Push them to explain why anything is good. If they don't go beyond ad campaigns or can't get specific, fail them. Lauchengco's hard test:

> If the candidate only goes through the four P's (product, price, promotion, place) without any original thinking, I consider this a fail.

**How they deal with rapid change.** Switch the competitor or the situation mid-discussion. Watch how they recalibrate.

**Openness to new information.** Introduce a shift: a new target customer, a different constraint. Watch whether their recommendation changes.

**Ability to manage constraints.** "If you only had a $250,000 budget, would it change what you do?" If the answer is "no, I'd do the same thing but slower," that's a poor answer. Good PMMs constrain their way to different plays.

## Calibration

Entry-level PMM candidates: one or two in ten are genuinely dynamic marketing thinkers. You'll interview a lot to find one.

Director-plus with a good recruiter: one in five, sometimes one in three.

If your pass rate is higher than that, your bar is too low. Lauchengco's instinct is to hire few and hire slow.

## Red flags for leaders

**Formulaic answers.** They've rehearsed, they're not thinking.

**No POV shift when you push back or introduce new data.** They're performing confidence rather than holding genuine views that can update.

**Polished on prepared plan, weak off-script.** They can present but they can't think.

**No evidence of results tied to business impact.** Campaigns launched; business unchanged.

**Industry expert with a fixed mindset.** They know the current playbook so well they can't see when it's the wrong playbook.

## The bias trap to avoid

Don't ask candidates "What are three things you think we could do better in our marketing?"

You already have answers in your head. You'll measure the candidate against your answers. You'll penalize them for insights you don't share and reward them for insights you do. It's a correlation check on you, not a thinking check on them.

Discussing outside companies removes this bias. That's why the signature question uses someone else's marketing, not your own.

## Applying this reference

When the user is hiring a PMM, walk them through:

1. The stage they're hiring for. Early-stage favors curious; mature favors collaborative.
2. The skills rubric above, with one or two priority dimensions emphasized.
3. The signature question, with the corollary and the probing moves.
4. The calibration numbers, so they don't settle for a one-in-ten candidate just because they've already done ten interviews.

Don't produce a standard "top ten PMM interview questions" list. Produce a rubric, a signature question, and a set of probes. That's the actual working interview kit.
