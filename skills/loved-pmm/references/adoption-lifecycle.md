# Technology Adoption Life Cycle

Chapter 15. The curve everyone in tech learned in business school, applied honestly to PMM decisions. This reference is short because the discipline is in the application, not the theory.

## The curve

Innovators, early adopters, early majority, late majority, laggards. Each segment has distinct psychographics and distinct reasons for buying. Lauchengco's reminder: "These adoption cycles typically take 7 to 10 or more years to move through." If a company expects mainstream adoption in year two, either the category is unusually fast-moving or the expectation is wrong.

## Why this matters for PMM, not just PM

Product managers move faster through the early segments because their job is to define jobs-to-be-done and the problems the product solves. They can treat segments somewhat interchangeably at the requirements level.

The PMM can't. PMM's job is to find addressable customers most likely to buy given where the product and the category actually sit on the curve. Positioning, pricing, channels, and messaging all change by segment. Treating innovators and early majority as one group is the single most common PMM mistake in growth-stage companies.

## Segmentation variables in modern marketing

Demographics, psychographics, industry verticals, technographics (what tech they already use), firmographics (size, geography, industry), behaviors (likelihood to buy), intent signals, product usage, browsing history, account-based actions.

Use several of these in combination. A single-variable segment is almost always too broad.

## The warning

> Watch the tendency to overgeneralize customer segments.

SMB / mid-market / enterprise is not a marketable segment. Those are company-size buckets used by finance and sales comp. They say nothing about what the buyer is trying to do.

A marketable segment looks like this: "a mid-level supply-chain manager willing to adopt new technology who directs purchases across at least 50 vendors." For consumers: not "everyone who uses the Internet" but "a word-lover curious about new words and language." Specific enough that you know where to find them, what to say to them, and what they'll do when they hear it.

## The iPhone walk-through

Apple is the clearest worked example of matching activity to segment across a decade.

- **Innovators.** Launch price $599. Priced to keep adoption exclusive among people who fan future adoption. Lauchengco's friend Mike stood in line day one.
- **Early adopters.** Months later, an OS upgrade dropped price to $499. Lauchengco and techie friends.
- **Early majority.** iPhone 3G at $199 with AT&T exclusive and the App Store launch. Ten million apps downloaded the first weekend. Lauchengco's siblings in finance, military, commercial production got phones. iPhone 4 on Verizon with FaceTime and Retina brought her mother-in-law in.
- **Late majority and laggards.** iPhone 5/6/7 era, seven years in. Her mother, a German immigrant resistant to technology, got one for Mother's Day. What won her over: digital-camera convenience and FaceTime with grandkids. "Very old features at that point."

Apple's long-term goal was adoption by people like Lauchengco's mother. They didn't start there. Every pricing, distribution, and messaging move matched the segment they were currently trying to cross.

## Superhuman example (Rahul Vohra)

Lauchengco uses this to show counterintuitive PMM in early-adopter mode.

- Position: "the fastest email experience ever made."
- Refer-a-friend jumped the beta line. Waitlist grew to tens of thousands.
- Qualified users before accepting into beta.
- Monetization at onboarding start. No free trial.
- Required onboarding call.

"They said 'sorry, not yet' to any early customer they weren't sure would help them succeed."

The lesson: when targeting early adopters who set the tone for early majority, restrict access. Let the segment feel earned. Don't accelerate to mass access until you've won the right beachhead.

## B2B note

In cybersecurity, the CISO is the sought-after decision-maker. Palo Alto Networks can sell directly to CISOs because of brand, existing CISO programs, and a large sales team. An unknown startup can't. An unknown startup's play is to find users feeling daily pain who can influence the CISO. Same final buyer, totally different route.

## Online-backup anchor

Initial target: "anyone whose money was green." Paid radio-host endorsements worked but became too expensive, and the customer base skewed old (senior citizens). No word of mouth, no reviews. The fix: shift to early-30s NPR listeners with more to lose. Tight segment, better loops.

Lesson: a working channel with the wrong segment is a trap. It prints revenue today and starves the brand for tomorrow.

## Applying the life cycle in planning

When a user is planning GTM for the next quarter, force a conversation about which segment they're actually addressing. Ask:

1. Who is already buying? What segment are they? Get concrete, like the supply-chain-manager example above.
2. Which segment is the next one to cross? What evidence do you have that they're ready?
3. What's different about that next segment: prioritization of the problem, willingness to try new tech, risk tolerance, buying process, channels they trust?
4. What pricing, packaging, message, and channel changes does the crossing require?

If the answers are the same as last year, the company is standing still on the curve. That's sometimes correct (still working the current segment) and sometimes a slow crisis (market has moved and they haven't). Either way, naming it is the PMM's job.

## Don't over-lean on the curve

Lauchengco uses the curve pragmatically. She doesn't invoke Geoffrey Moore's chasm or ride the full Crossing the Chasm playbook, and she warns against treating segments as a linear conveyor. Some products never cross to early majority and shouldn't. Some jump unusually fast because of a catalyst (pandemic, regulatory change, platform shift). The curve is a framing tool, not a prophecy.
