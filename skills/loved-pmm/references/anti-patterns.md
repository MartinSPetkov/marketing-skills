# PMM Anti-Patterns

Collected from throughout the book, organized by where they surface. When a user describes one of these by symptom, you can name the pattern and point to the fix.

## PMM role anti-patterns (Ch 7)

**Strong on style, soft on substance.** Everything looks professional. The collateral is on-brand. Saves time and budget argument gets made. But GTM teams keep asking what the product does. Prospects go elsewhere for "real" info. PMs spend too much time on field enablement.

*Fix:* Measure PMM on substance before style. Substance means: can sales answer "what does it do?" without calling the PMM. Is messaging specific enough that a non-employee can tell your product apart from a competitor's?

**Stuck in technical weeds.** Accurate but no clear position. Every page is a feature list. Competition defines what your product means because you haven't.

*Fix:* Force positioning first. Then the feature lists fall in under pillars. See `references/messaging-canvas.md`.

**Functioning like a service.** "Give them what they ask for" orientation. PMM responds to requests. Leadership still wondering if PGTM is working.

*Fix:* Build a PGTM Canvas (`references/product-gtm-canvas.md`) so PMM activity traces back to strategy. Say no to requests that don't tie in.

**Insufficient dedicated PMM.** Under-resourced. PMs doing sales enablement to fill the gap. Results from a "false understanding of how powerful product marketing can be when done well."

*Fix:* Show the cost of no PMM. Deal cycles lengthen; win rate drops; launches miss. Build the business case for headcount, not the political case.

## Product partnership anti-patterns (Ch 8)

**What product ships, sales can't sell.** The archetypal failure. Tech-first vacuum. Engineers build. Sales can't explain it. Customers don't buy.

*Fix:* PMM as a partner in product planning, empowered to challenge plans out of line with market realities. The empowerment matters. A PMM who can raise concerns but can't escalate them has no real role.

**Product ships at a terrible time to market it.** Announced the week of a major industry event. Holiday week. Apple keynote day. Anything that steals all the oxygen.

*Fix:* PMM represents market and customer perspective in timing decisions before dates lock. Use the PGTM Canvas to surface timing conflicts a quarter ahead.

**PMs pulled into collateral or sales support constantly.** Usually because the PMM doesn't know the product well enough to speak for it.

*Fix:* PM invests upfront in getting PMM up to speed. Not one meeting. A structured onboarding across the product. Then PM can offload.

## Marketing partnership anti-patterns (Ch 9)

**Campaigns perform but product isn't positioned.** Campaign KPIs hit. Click-through rates up. Nobody can tell you what the company stands for six months later.

*Fix:* PMM in campaign planning, with veto power over creative that wins clicks but undermines positioning.

**"Future state" messaging too far ahead of current reality.** Aspiration without product to back it. Detracts from credibility. Prospects who buy based on aspirational messaging churn on first product contact.

*Fix:* PMM finds the line between aspirational and current and defends it. See the Loudcloud cautionary example in `references/category-strategy.md`.

**Creative that pushes the edge but doesn't connect.** Wins awards, loses customers. A creative exercise that escaped the marketing team's control.

*Fix:* Test with customers before deciding. Two or three variants, real medium, small budget. The answer is usually boringly obvious when you test.

## Sales partnership anti-patterns (Ch 10)

**Marketing as a service to sales.** Activities focus on immediate deal support. Strategic work gets crowded out.

*Fix:* Examine what moves toward PGTM goals. Use data to prioritize over loudest voice. Sales requests still get handled, but not all of them.

**Lack of awareness before sales engages.** "Spray and pray" prospecting. Costs a lot, converts little. Sales feels that marketing isn't helping; marketing feels that sales isn't targeting.

*Fix:* Targeted and ABM approaches coordinated with the customer journey. If sales is calling cold, marketing should have warmed the account first.

**Mavericks damaging positioning.** Reps writing their own decks, their own emails, their own product descriptions. Each variation erodes the positioning slightly.

*Fix:* Co-create tools with reps so they own them. Then hold reps accountable for using them. Top-down enforcement without bottom-up input produces shelf-ware.

## Pricing anti-patterns (Ch 17)

**Cost-plus pricing for software.** The product costs pennies to run. You priced on cost, not value. You're leaving 5x to 20x on the table.

*Fix:* Pricing is value engineering. Anchor price to perceived value and willingness to pay, not to cost.

**Entry-level package stripped to nothing.** The "good" in good-better-best has so few features that no one would want it. Customers bounce.

*Fix:* Entry-level should include must-haves. The buyer reaction should be "look at all I get for this price," not "this is a toy."

**Sales ownership of pricing.** Every deal discounts. The next deal discounts from the last one. Floor prices become ceilings.

*Fix:* Sales input on pricing is essential. Sales ownership is not. PMM holds guardrails; sales negotiates inside them.

## Release and launch anti-patterns (Ch 12)

**Sprint, release, and launch used interchangeably.** Teams talk past each other. GTM doesn't know what's actually going public when.

*Fix:* Define the three terms on the team. Use them with discipline. See `references/release-scale.md`.

**Release Scale built with hypothetical releases.** Calibration drifts within two quarters.

*Fix:* Anchor levels to real past releases. "Level 3 looks like the X release from March."

**Release Scale used only by product.** Sales and marketing don't know the levels. Predictable result: level inflation, because sales argues every release is Level 5.

*Fix:* Train sales and marketing on the scale. Make it a shared document.

## Messaging anti-patterns (Ch 5, 21, 22)

**Formula output as published copy.** "For [target customer] who [statement of need], [product name] is a [category] that [key benefit]; unlike [alternative], our product [differentiation]" taken from the working doc and pasted onto the website.

*Fix:* Formula as input, not output. Do the discovery (`references/messaging-process.md`). Write for humans.

**Messaging from the company's POV instead of the customer's.** "We empower..." "Our platform..." "Our innovative..."

*Fix:* Listen to customers. Use their phrases. See Expensify vs Concur in `examples/expensify-vs-concur-messaging.md`.

**Testing in a Google Doc.** Messaging that reads fine on a slide falls apart on a real landing page.

*Fix:* Test in the real medium. Landing page, email, sales slide. The medium reveals the jargon.

**Synonym stacking.** "A comprehensive, robust, sophisticated platform."

*Fix:* Pick one word. Cut the rest.

**Aspirational messaging for a late-majority product.** "We're reinventing the future of X." The product is selling to a late-majority buyer who wants reliability, not revolution.

*Fix:* Right message, right time. See the stage-appropriate messaging in `references/category-strategy.md`.

## Metrics anti-patterns (Ch 13)

**MQLs as the PMM metric.** Marketing metric wearing a PMM nametag.

*Fix:* PMM metrics are longer-horizon. Category leadership, competitive win rate, organic evangelism, CAC trend, NPS. See `references/pmm-metrics.md`.

**No competitive win-rate tracking.** You don't know if your positioning works against the alternatives customers consider.

*Fix:* Track win rate by competitor. When it drops, run the full-stack examination (training, tools, process, playbook, mix, messaging, positioning, pricing, packaging, evangelism).

**Brand investment killed because demand-gen metrics move faster.** Six-week campaign beats a year-long brand effort in a quarterly scorecard. Brand loses.

*Fix:* Get brand metrics on the scorecard before the budget debate.

## Org and leadership anti-patterns (Ch 26)

**Full-scale reorganization before piloting the new model.** Disruptive, expensive, and risky. Often fails.

*Fix:* Pair your best PM with your best PMM on the most important segment. Try the model first. Then scale.

**Every function assuming PMM fills its own gap.** Product wants adoption support. Sales wants collateral. Marketing wants campaign copy. PMM becomes all of the above and none well.

*Fix:* Define PMM scope explicitly with each partner function. Write it down. Revisit quarterly.

**Hiring for polish instead of thinking.** See `references/hiring-pmm.md`. The crisp presenter who can't adapt off-script is a common PMM hiring mistake.

*Fix:* Run the signature interview question. Probe off-script. Prefer the curious candidate at early stages.

## How to use this reference

When a user describes a PMM problem, check this list for a match by symptom. If you find one, name the pattern and point to the fix. Don't solve the symptom; solve the pattern underneath.

Don't use this list as a checklist. It's not comprehensive. It's the patterns Lauchengco saw often enough to name. The book has many more situational cases that don't fit a clean pattern.
