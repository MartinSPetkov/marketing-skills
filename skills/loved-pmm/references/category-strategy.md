# Category Strategy

Chapter 24. When PMM thinks about categories, there are two moves: create a new one, or redefine an existing one. Lauchengco has a strong opinion about which usually wins.

## The central claim

> There are far more examples of great success stories from those who redefine existing categories than of those who defined something truly new.

The category-creation playbook (Play Bigger, "category design," etc.) is over-indexed in Silicon Valley. Redefinition is less glamorous, more common, and more likely to succeed.

## Create new vs redefine existing (Table 24.1)

| Created a new category | Redefined an existing category |
|---|---|
| Amazon (the store) | Google → surpassed Yahoo |
| Amazon Web Services | Apple iPhone → surpassed Blackberry |
| Netflix | Facebook → surpassed MySpace, Friendster |
| RedHat (acquired by IBM) | Salesforce → surpassed Siebel |
|  | Slack (acquired by Salesforce) → surpassed Hipchat (acquired by Atlassian) |
|  | Spotify → surpassed Pandora |
|  | SpaceX → now used by NASA |
|  | Microsoft → nearly every business it's in |
|  | Tesla → redefined everything in electric cars |

The right column is longer than the left. There's the answer.

## Why redefinition is easier

> You have that "You are here" marker from which to anchor conversations.

Customers already know what the old category is. They have words for it, comparison criteria, budget categories, buying committees. Your job is to move them inside a frame they already understand, not build the frame from nothing.

Category creation has to build the frame. That takes a decade, budget, and timing that's rarely reproducible.

## What category creation actually takes

Lauchengco's honest list for the rare cases where category creation is the right play:

- An industry shift (real, not marketed).
- Others following you (an emerging cohort of competitors validates the category).
- Great product strategy.
- Great business strategy.
- Great marketing strategy.
- Exceptional leadership.
- Resources.
- Luck.
- Consistent execution over time.
- Ample external evidence of a shift in customers' world.

If you're missing two or three of these, you're not creating a category. You're posturing about one.

## The Loudcloud example (cautionary)

At Loudcloud, the cloud/utility computing message came too early. No common language existed. No industry groundswell. The message was right; the timing was wrong. The company shifted to EDS services (Opsware) and was later sold to HP.

Lesson: being right and being early are the same thing when the market doesn't come along. If the timing isn't there, reposition to a category the market already has words for. You can keep the vision; you can't keep the category claim.

## The Office Internet anchor (positive)

Microsoft Office focus groups at launch of the Internet-ready features showed Internet capabilities weren't near the top of value stacks. Microsoft led with them anyway. Right call.

The market was about to discover how much Internet features would matter. The messaging pulled ahead of current demand but not so far that customers couldn't follow. Within two years, Internet-ready became a buying criterion.

Lesson: you can lead the market by one step. Maybe one and a half. You can't lead by five. Loudcloud led by five and ran out of runway.

## The category-fit test

Lauchengco:

> Competition isn't a crowd; it's confirmation.

If there's no competition in your category, that's often a bad sign, not a good one. It means customers haven't bought anything like this yet. The longer they haven't, the harder your PGTM is. A crowded category is a funded category.

Use this as a reality check when a founder tells you "we have no real competition." The correct follow-up is not admiration. It's: "Who are customers buying instead when they're not buying you?" There's always an alternative. Find it. That's your category.

## Right message at the right time

> The earlier in a product's life you are, the more you must tell a story using the pain and problems that make you necessary. The more mature you are, messaging can be more aspirational.

Three concrete implications:

1. Early-stage products should lead with problem specificity. Problem-led messaging outperforms aspirational messaging in nine out of ten early-stage ad tests.
2. Mid-stage products can lead with differentiation. The customer knows they need a solution; the question is why yours.
3. Late-stage, category-leading products can lead with aspiration. Nike doesn't sell shoes; it sells athletic identity. Apple doesn't sell phones; it sells a way of being in the world. But they earned that altitude with decades of problem-led and differentiation-led work.

Don't skip stages. Aspirational messaging from a 20-person startup reads as pretension. Problem-led messaging from Apple reads as tired.

## Leveraging PMs for category story

PMs hold the vision and the strategy. They're technical enough to catch where the story outruns what the product can do.

Example: if AI is where your category is going, show how AI makes specific features better. Don't lead with "we're an AI company" if the AI is a single feature. The PM will tell you the real story; use it.

## Leveraging sales for category story

The CEO / head of marketing / head of sales test described in `references/messaging-process.md`. Each writes a home-page message. Each version tests for a week. In Lauchengco's case, the sales head's version beat the CEO's by over 1000%. Sales hears what customers actually say. That language is the category language.

## How to advise on category work

When a user asks about category strategy, run these questions:

1. Is there an existing category customers are already buying from? If yes, default to redefining it.
2. What's the "You are here" marker for customers coming to your product? If you can't name one, your messaging is starting from zero every time.
3. Who are the alternatives customers actually consider? If the answer is "nobody," dig again. There's always an alternative (doing nothing, a spreadsheet, a competitor with a bad name).
4. Is there an industry shift with evidence? What's the evidence? If the evidence is "I've been hearing about it on LinkedIn," the shift isn't there yet.
5. What's your current position on the adoption curve? Early adopters tolerate new categories; early majority rejects them.

Only if the answers push clearly toward category creation do you pursue that path. Otherwise, redefine.

## Don't mistake category creation for brand differentiation

Two different moves that get collapsed. Category creation says "here is a new kind of thing." Brand differentiation says "here is a better version of the existing thing." Most companies think they're doing the first when they're really doing the second, and it shows up in messaging that tries too hard to invent vocabulary.

If your buyers already have words for what you do, use those words. Your differentiation is in what's different about your specific product, not in a new category label.
