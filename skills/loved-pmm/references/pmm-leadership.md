# Leading and Organizing PMM

Chapter 26. Where PMM reports, how to structure the team, and how to reboot a struggling PMM org.

## The Mala Sharma anchor

Mala Sharma trained at Unilever India in brand management. She knew weekly sales figures and cost structures like a virtual GM. When she moved to Silicon Valley, she found nobody tracked business accountability that way. At Adobe Photoshop, she showed up at every meeting to understand market opportunity better than anyone. She modeled strategic PMM until perceptions shifted. She hired outside pure-tech backgrounds: consumer packaged goods, tech, management consulting.

Lesson: the strongest PMM leaders often come from disciplines where business accountability is assumed. Their arrival changes the bar. A PMM leader who cares only about launch logistics will never get a seat at the table where strategy is decided.

## Reporting decision: two factors

### Factor 1: business problem

**Organize PMM under marketing when:**
- The product is established.
- Growth requires market focus.
- Micro-segmentation is needed.
- Partnerships and suites dominate the strategy.
- PMM works as an overlay across products.
- Market and customer dynamics drive GTM thinking more than product evolution does.

**Organize PMM under product when:**
- The company is in continuous-development mode.
- The product is highly technical.
- The company struggles to communicate well about its product.
- Reducing friction between PMM and product info to GTM is the priority.
- PMMs need to be the voice of market back to product.

### Factor 2: leader capacity

Lauchengco's honest second factor:

> If you're lucky enough to have an extraordinary leader with a unique combination of superpowers, take advantage.

A market-savvy CPO who thinks more strategically than the CMO can justify PMM reporting to product even in a mature market. The reverse also holds.

Don't apply Factor 1 in isolation. The org chart question is partly a business question and partly a people question. Honest assessment of who your best leader is, is a legitimate input.

## Role scope

Early stage: generalists are valuable. Specialization creates gaps you can't afford to staff.

Mature stage: specialize around vertical markets, marketing or distribution channels, customer segments. A single generalist PMM spread across six products underperforms three specialist PMMs split by segment.

Don't force specialization too early and don't cling to generalists too late.

## Rebooting a struggling PMM org

> Don't start with a full-scale reorganization.

Instead, pair your very best product leader with your very best PMM leader in the most important market segment. Try the model. Adjust. Then roll out.

A full reorg before you've proven the operating model is a multi-quarter gamble. A single paired unit as a pilot lets you test whether the structure works without risking the whole team.

## Key dynamics to address in any reboot

**PMM <> PM.** Build trust around customer and market knowledge. Observe interactions firsthand. If PMs don't trust PMM's market read, PMM work gets discounted no matter how good it is.

**PMM <> GTM.** Establish clear ways to discuss when sales or marketing feel what PMM gave them isn't working. Without a channel for that conversation, feedback goes stale or political.

**PMM <> Execs.** Most execs don't know what PMM does. Define scope relative to company priorities. Measure short-term and long-term. Expect to have to teach this more than once.

Missing any one of these dynamics, the reboot stalls. Fix all three in parallel, or don't bother rebooting.

## Project Aristotle: team norms

Google's research on team performance found five factors correlated with high-performing teams. Lauchengco imports them directly into PMM because PMM is unusually cross-functional.

1. **Psychological safety.** Confidence to take risks with questions, ideas, and disagreement.
2. **Dependability.** Reliable quality work on time. Accepting accountability.
3. **Structure and clarity.** Clear expectations.
4. **Meaning.** Purpose in one's work.
5. **Impact.** Feeling the contribution matters.

Why it matters for PMM:

> They operate so cross-functionally. They must feel confident to influence outcomes with teams where they don't share a reporting structure.

A PMM without psychological safety can't push back on a PM who's wrong about the market. A PMM without impact doesn't know if anything they said actually changed anything. Either breakdown ends a career fast.

## Leader behaviors that produce the five norms

Lauchengco's short list for PMM leaders specifically.

- Make quiet people feel heard.
- Speak last instead of first in group discussions.
- Embrace different POVs actively, not as a platitude.
- Don't defend against opinions you disagree with; steelman them.
- Candor. Respect. Clarity. Willingness to have hard conversations.

These read like leadership basics. They are. PMM leaders fail the basics more often than average because PMM is often staffed by people promoted for individual-contributor messaging skill, not for management skill.

## Structural decisions a PMM leader owns

Beyond reporting line, a PMM leader decides:

- Ratio of PMMs to PMs (see `references/partner-collaboration.md`, the 1:2.5 average).
- Alignment pattern: by product, by suite, by segment, by vertical, by channel. See the five patterns in that same reference.
- Shared services (content, analyst relations, competitive intel) vs embedded specialists.
- How specialization ramps as the company grows.
- Where PMM sits on OKR scorecards (see `references/pmm-metrics.md`).

Each decision feeds the others. Changing one without the others usually breaks the system.

## Advising a PMM leader

When the user is a PMM leader asking about structure, ask:

1. What's the business problem we're trying to solve with this reorg? Not "what's the right structure," but "what's broken that a structure fix would help?"
2. Who are your best two leaders? What would pairing them on your hardest segment produce?
3. What's the current reporting line, and what's specifically failing about it?
4. What do your execs think PMM does? If the answers vary, the role definition isn't set.

Don't jump to an org chart. Fix the definition and the pilot first.
