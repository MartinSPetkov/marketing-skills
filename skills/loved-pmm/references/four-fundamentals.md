# The Four Fundamentals

Lauchengco's central frame. Every PMM task, role, and deliverable maps to one of four fundamentals. A strong PMM org has all four; a struggling one is usually starved on one or two.

## Fundamental 1. Ambassador — Connect Customer and Market Insights

The PMM is the product org's ambassador to the market and the market's ambassador to the product org. Everything the PMM does downstream rests on this.

Scope of work:

- Segment customers and know their frustrations.
- Map the journey from first awareness to raving fan.
- Identify "watering holes" where influence concentrates (forums, podcasts, Slack groups, conferences, analyst coverage).
- Track competitive dynamics without over-rotating to competitive response.
- Know the product deeply enough that the product team trusts you.

### Signature technique: Market Sensing (Ch 3)

Four baseline practices, done always:

1. Direct customer interaction, ideally weekly.
2. A standard set of open-ended questions.
3. Reflect insights into product and GTM discussions.
4. Write down the most important insights so they can be shared and used.

The open-ended question set:

- What are they trying to do?
- Do they recognize and prioritize this problem?
- What is motivating them to solve the problem?
- What compels them to take action?
- What in this product delivers the most value?
- Who is most likely to value and buy this product?
- What starts the journey toward acquiring the product?
- How might a product get discovered and become more desired over the journey?
- How might we reduce friction in acquiring the product?
- What do people need to see or hear to become customers?
- How can we delight customers so much that they want to talk about the product with others?

### Third-party insights

Google Search Trends for relative word usage. Content services showing top-read content on a subject. Internal or agency customer insights, research, and analytics teams. Social listening.

### Insight filter

Before adding anything to the PMM's insight inventory, ask: "Will this help a team make a decision or tradeoff on what to say or do next?" If yes, it's additive. If no, archive it.

### Insights vs requirements

Insights are inputs for smarter decisions. They're not product requirements. How any insight affects the roadmap is the PM's call. PMMs who confuse these two will burn trust fast.

### Four artifacts for customer insights

Different functions use different customer documents for different jobs:

- **JTBD (Jobs to Be Done)**: product team.
- **Personas**: design and product.
- **ICP (Ideal Customer Profile)**: sales.
- **Customer segmentations**: marketing.

Don't collapse them. They serve different purposes.

### Competition

"Think of this as chess; move ahead of your competition, don't just respond." Meet challenge with challenge. Beware over-rotating to competitive response; it lets the competitor set your agenda.

### Dropbox example

Julie Herendeen (VP Global Marketing) had her entire team visit customers. They discovered customers valued the freedom to work with whomever they wanted however they wanted. That insight rewrote messaging, reshaped channels, and changed ad campaigns. Ambassador work directly produced Storyteller and Strategist output.

## Fundamental 2. Strategist — Direct Your Product's Go-to-Market

The PMM defines a clear Product Go-to-Market (PGTM) plan whose strategies align directly with business goals. Strategies act as guardrails, not activity lists.

### Key terms (learn them, use them)

- **GTM Engine**: the sum total of all marketing and sales machinery bringing products to market.
- **Marketing Strategy** (aka GTM Strategy): orchestrates the marketing elements in the GTM engine.
- **Product Go-to-Market (PGTM)**: the unique purview of product marketing. The path for a particular product to go to market. This is the PMM's domain.
- **Distribution Strategy** (aka GTM Strategy, Business Model): the chosen model. Direct sales, inside sales, channel partners, direct-to-professional, trial/freemium, or product-led growth.
- **Channel Strategy**: either distribution form or marketing mix, depending on who's using the term.
- **Product Strategy**: connects business objectives and product vision to work done by product teams.
- **Business Goals or Objectives**: specific, measurable desired achievements over a time period.

When the user conflates PGTM with GTM Strategy, clarify. They're related but distinct. GTM Strategy is about the engine; PGTM is about a specific product's path through it.

### Marketing strategy starter questions

Before writing strategies, ask these:

- Is third-party validation important for credibility?
- What kind of customers are you trying to acquire and how fast?
- Where do those customers spend time professionally or personally?
- Are you trying to educate the space?
- What are the product's strengths?
- Are there particular trends that present opportunities in your category?
- Does someone else already have established relationships with the customers you're trying to reach?
- What is the preferred way to adopt new products or technology for your customers?

### Six strategy themes every PGTM falls into

Strategy themes, not strategies themselves. Pick the ones that fit.

1. Enable growth to hit a revenue or business goal.
2. Improve conversion of specific customers.
3. Generate awareness, improve discovery, or build a brand.
4. Define, reshape, or lead a category, ecosystem, or platform.
5. Engender customer validation, loyalty, or evangelism.
6. Find and develop new customer segments, partners, and programs.

### Pocket 5.0 example

Strategies were "grow a loyal user base, define and lead the category, and leverage partnerships for growth." The team used these three as guardrails to judge each idea. One output: a Pocket Matters event at a San Francisco wine bar with press, partners, and ten customers. Small, specific, on-strategy.

## Fundamental 3. Storyteller — Shape How the World Thinks About Your Product

The PMM shapes the market's perception of the product. Two concepts, often confused:

- **Positioning** = the place your product holds in the minds of customers. How they know what you do and how you differ. Long-game.
- **Messaging** = the key things you say to reinforce your positioning. Short-game.

Both matter. The distinction matters. A company can have great messaging and zero positioning, and it will churn as soon as messaging shifts.

### Positioning = Your Actions + Others'

70% of decision-making happens beyond a company's control: comparison sites, reviews, ratings, social posts, forums, third-party content, employee buzz. Every PGTM activity reinforces (or undermines) positioning. Positioning is never only what the company says.

### Formulas are input, not output

The standard positioning template ("For [target customer] who [statement of need], [product name] is a [category] that [key benefit]; unlike [primary competitive alternative], our product [statement of primary differentiation]") is fine as a **thinking** tool. It becomes slop when used as the **published output**.

See `references/messaging-process.md` for the CAST filter and the better discovery-led process.

## Fundamental 4. Evangelist — Enable Others to Tell the Story

The PMM systematically enables influence through others. Customers, analysts, reviewers, press, partners, superfans, employees, internal sales reps.

### Quizlet example

For its first 10 years, Quizlet spent $0 on marketing. #quizlet, #thanksquizlet, graduation mortarboards. By the end of that run, one in two US high-school students had used it.

### Evangelism vs Promotion (Ch 6, Table 6.1)

| Promoting Product | Enables Evangelism |
|---|---|
| Customer case studies stating key challenges and results | Interest-grabbing customer stories told via video, UGC, photo-based social |
| Product demo walking through features by product screens | Demo through a day-in-the-life of a customer |
| Sales deck about company, product, marketecture, features | Slides showing what's changed in the world that makes the need urgent, and how a customer like the one being pitched found value |

Case studies still have a role. The point is they can't be the only way customer stories get told.

### Who to enable, in order

1. Direct sales first (if you have them). They need a playbook.
2. Analysts from research firms. They set category expectations.
3. App store editors and Tier 1 publications. They move traffic.
4. Community: switching prospects want a forum; existing customers want recognition; pundits have cadences to hit.

### Word for Windows 95 example

A detailed evaluation guide for reviewers and sales. In-person meetings with top pundits. In-depth sales field training. A beta program producing reference customers that fed direct mail and ads. "Word couldn't claim leadership; pundits, reviewers, partners, and customers had to give it to us."

## How the four connect

The four fundamentals are not a checklist. They compound.

- Ambassador work produces the insight base. Without it, Strategy is guessing and Storytelling is company-POV slop.
- Strategist work sets the guardrails that Storytelling and Evangelism execute against. Without it, messaging drifts and campaigns misfire.
- Storyteller work produces the language the company and its market both use. Without it, Evangelism has nothing to hand to sales, analysts, or customers.
- Evangelist work makes the story travel. Without it, a great story dies inside the company.

When diagnosing a struggling PMM org, ask which fundamentals are strong and which are starved. The fix is almost always to rebuild the starved one rather than to push harder on the strong one.
