# Campaigns Beyond Product

Chapter 18. Most marketing plans orbit the product. The product ships and the campaign tells people. Lauchengco argues that a lot of the highest-leverage campaign moments aren't triggered by product at all.

## The working definition

> A campaign is a specific collection of coordinated actions to address a specific market opportunity or challenge.

Three ideas packed into one sentence: coordinated (not scattered), specific (not generic), and opportunity or challenge (not product release). Product releases are campaigns too, but they're not the only kind that deserves the label.

## Six campaign triggers worth coordinated effort

Lauchengco names these specifically because each recurs in her consulting work.

1. **Leverage a black-swan event.** Pandemic, regulatory shift, platform deprecation, economic shock. The market's attention is already on a topic. The question is what you say while people are listening.

2. **Target a particular micro vertical.** Sole-practitioner accounting firms, cardiology departments in community hospitals, municipal water utilities. Micro-vertical campaigns outperform broad vertical campaigns because the language, watering holes, and buying process are tighter.

3. **Amplify a singular company event.** Funding round, acquisition, going public, major hire, office opening. The event gives permission for press, customer emails, and partner outreach that wouldn't have landed otherwise.

4. **Activate a dormant or existing customer segment.** Customers who bought once and stopped, or customers on an old SKU. Reactivation is almost always cheaper than acquisition, and PMM is better positioned than demand-gen to diagnose why they went quiet.

5. **Shift brand perception.** The product hasn't changed, but the story about it has to. Done well, this is a months-long campaign with coordinated content, third-party validation, and sales retraining.

6. **Convert users of a competitive product.** Targeted switching campaigns. Works when the competitor has a recent stumble (price hike, outage, acquisition uncertainty) or when your product has crossed a feature threshold that makes switching viable.

## Key moves when product isn't the catalyst

When a campaign isn't triggered by a product launch, the temptation is to invent a launch anyway. Don't. The moves below are the alternatives.

**Invest in brand emotionality.** Tech companies underinvest here. Customers aspire to be something, and brand work is where that aspiration lives. Feature pages and spec sheets don't do this job.

**Deepen sales-marketing collaboration.** Vertical campaigns win or lose based on whether sales is actually ready to run the plays the campaign implies. That means smaller, hyper-focused, customized activities, not a giant agency-produced spectacle.

**Examine the customer journey.** What parts of the journey are underserved? Peloton built in-mall pods before heavy digital. NPS reach-outs can generate reviews and testimonials. Outdoor, radio, TV still work for some categories when used with intent.

**Enable evangelism from customers.** Not a referral program mechanic. A slower build: start by making customers successful. Then give successful customers stages and stories. A referral program is a payout; evangelism is a relationship.

**Activate community.** Forums, meetups, expert armies, customer councils. Lauchengco's warning: "The mistake most make with community is they focus on tools or events, the scaffolding." The work is the relationships that live inside the scaffolding, not the scaffolding itself.

## The Modern Hire case study

Worth quoting in detail because it's a full-frame example of a non-product campaign.

**Context.** HR Tech company. COVID shutdown. Competitors pivoted to free video interviewing. Modern Hire had two weeks to strategize, then decided against free video interviewing. Their campaign theme: "elevate the importance of better candidate experiences and smarter hiring decisions." Tagline: "Let's get to work."

**Sales pre-campaign prep.** Educated on messaging. Email templates, scripts, talking points with empathy baked in. Competitive sell sheet versus conferencing tools (Zoom, Teams, Meet). Client case studies. Updated partnership slides.

**Campaign inventory, in order of use.**
- Home-page refresh.
- COVID-specific landing page.
- Expanded product pages and industry pages.
- Weekly CEO blog.
- Daily social posts.
- Six-part video series.
- Twelve-part email nurture.
- Weekly podcast.
- Weekly webinar.
- LinkedIn and Google paid ads.
- ABM via LinkedIn InMail.
- Hiring-manager and candidate guides and e-books.

**Sales during campaign.** Webinar replay links, weekly press roundups, relevant case studies, client social shout-outs, rapid-response packages for hot prospects.

**Results.** Record SQLs and accepted leads. Website traffic up 100%. SEO up 100%. New wins including Macy's, Stitch Fix, BCBS, Target, Cargill. Double-digit revenue growth while competitors laid off.

**Why this worked.** Thoughtful then swift. Differentiators leveraged without being product-led. Customer-centric. Emphasis on value perception when the category went free around them. A broad mix, not any single tactic. Sales and marketing operating as extensions of each other.

## What this reference buys you

When a user asks for "a marketing campaign" and the product isn't launching anything soon, don't force a fake launch. Walk through the six triggers and ask which one is real right now. If none are, the honest answer may be "there's no campaign to run this quarter; work on positioning or brand instead." That answer is more useful than a calendar of tactics.

When a user is running a campaign, use the Modern Hire pattern as a reality check. Have they pre-loaded sales? Do they have a mix, or one hero tactic? Is the campaign tied to a specific opportunity or challenge, or is it product-brochure content with a campaign label stuck on top?
