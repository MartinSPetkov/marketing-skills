# The Product Go-to-Market Canvas

Chapter 19. The most-used artifact in the book. A one-page plan that aligns product milestones, marketing strategies, and key activities across the quarters of a year, all grounded in the customer and outside environment.

## The Bandwidth anchor story

Top 10 accounts growing slower than the next 50. The next 50 had different needs than the top 10. Product lead John Bell called a three-hour working session with sales, marketing, PMM, and BD. The output was the PGTM Canvas, owned by PMM.

What they discovered in the first session alone:
- No strong brand or position in the market.
- The fastest-growth segment was one sales knew little about.
- Digital assets didn't speak to high-growth segments.
- Sales frontline experience wasn't reaching product or PMM.
- No systematic learning from lost customers.
- Unclear ongoing value story after initial sale.

The canvas didn't fix those problems. It made them visible. That's most of what a canvas is for.

## What the canvas is

> A product go-to-market is like a puzzle. A product go-to-market canvas is like giving everyone the picture of what that puzzle should look like before anyone starts building it.

It's a guardrail document, not a comprehensive plan. Four borders do four jobs:

1. Show opportunities or misalignment quickly. Two squads staffed the same quarter with conflicting priorities? Visible on the page.
2. Keep everyone focused when improvising. A market shift lands and the team has to adapt; the canvas keeps the adaptation aligned to the goal instead of chaotic.
3. Show the most important activities at-a-glance. Not a calendar of 47 tactics, the handful that matter.
4. Show the why. Every activity has a line back to a strategy; every strategy has a line back to a business goal.

## The swim lanes (Figure 19.1)

Four rows, running top to bottom, laid out across quarters of a year (left to right).

1. **Customer / Outside Environment.** Customer reality. Plus: competitive announcements, conference events, major ecosystem releases (iOS updates, major cloud-provider updates), regulatory events. This row grounds everything else in what the market is actually doing that year.

2. **Product Milestones.** Major initiatives or commitments. Integrations. New platforms. Features for particular customer segments. Enough detail that the rest of the canvas can react to it. Not every sprint item.

3. **Marketing Strategies.** The guardrails. Not a list of campaigns. Two to five strategies, each traceable back to a business goal. See `references/four-fundamentals.md` for the six strategy themes a PGTM falls into.

4. **Key Activities.** Not a full checklist. Only activities others depend on or plan around. Quarterly sales training. New sales playbook rollout. Launching new pricing. Naming a key feature. Building a cross-suite demo.

Laid across four quarters, you see the flow of the year in one page.

## Who fills it in

Owned and driven by PMM. Kickoff discussion includes product, marketing, sales, customer success. The canvas is a cross-functional artifact with a single accountable owner.

If PMM doesn't exist yet at the company, the canvas still gets built. Either the CMO or the senior PM owns it until a PMM joins.

## The six-step build

Lauchengco's recommended flow. Run it in a three-hour working session.

**Setup: the three-hour meeting.** First hour: what's working, what's not, market realities, what must improve. Standard SWOT: strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, threats. The next two hours: fill in the canvas.

**Step 1: Customer and outside environment.** Ground everything in the customer perspective first. Add external events that might influence activities. Don't list every event. The goal is context, not a conference calendar.

**Step 2: List known product milestones.** Whatever is known from agile planning. Some quarters will be vague; that's fine. Label confidence, don't pretend certainty.

**Step 3: List marketing strategies.** Two to five. Each traceable back to a business goal. Strategies are guardrails: "what does it look like to be 'yes' on this strategy?"

**Step 4: List key activities.** Important dependencies and planning anchors. Use the SWOT output to leverage strengths and avoid marching into competitors' teeth.

Lauchengco: "This canvas is as much a communication tool as it is a planning one."

**Step 5: Work from the outside edges in.** Start with how you want the year to end. Work backwards. For early-stage companies, a "second half" parking lot is fine, but don't skip discussing it.

**Step 6: Revise.** Use time outside the meeting to think through detail. Don't fill every cell. Leave room for the unexpected. Revisit quarterly.

## Customer-first vs company-first (Table 19.1)

A recurring canvas tension: the product team's schedule vs. the customer's reality. Two examples from the book.

| Scenario | Company-first | Customer-first |
|---|---|---|
| Major product release ready March | Launch when product is done, turns out to fall on Spring Break | Work with product to find a launch date clear of major holiday breaks, aligned with an industry conference |
| New-customer-vertical campaign | Launches in December because sales worries about quota; vertical is busy with year-end planning and holidays | Year-end campaign with pricing incentive; new vertical campaign launches in January when vertical has capacity |

The canvas surfaces these tensions early enough to move dates. Without the canvas, the timing mismatch is invisible until it's too late.

Lauchengco's side note: "Sometimes who does the talking matters as much as what's being said. If a sales rep tells a customer about a coming product upgrade, it lands differently than if a customer success manager says the same thing." Use the canvas to assign the messenger too, not just the message.

## How the canvas is used after the kickoff

The kickoff builds it. The year uses it. Typical uses:

- Weekly team reference. "Why are we doing this campaign?" Pull up the canvas, find the strategy, find the activity.
- Quarterly revision. Rows shift. Strategies can shift if the market moved. Don't shift strategies because of one missed month.
- Onboarding new team members. Hand them the canvas before they read any docs. It's the fastest way to get someone up to speed.
- Executive updates. Walk the canvas, not a slide deck. The same artifact the team uses internally.

## Common failure modes

**Built once, filed away.** A canvas without quarterly revision is worse than no canvas, because the team thinks alignment exists when it doesn't.

**Filled in by PMM alone.** If only the PMM touches the canvas, it's a PMM planning document, not a cross-functional alignment tool. The value comes from the room being there when it's built.

**Strategies that are actually tactics.** "Launch the new feature in Q2" is a tactic. "Define and lead the category of X" is a strategy. If the row reads like a to-do list, it's the wrong altitude.

**Strategies with no link to business goals.** "Build brand awareness" on its own isn't a strategy. What awareness, by what measure, tied to what pipeline target? Without the line back to the business, leadership will defund it.

**Filling every cell.** The canvas is deliberately sparse. If every quarter of every row is full, you haven't prioritized.

## When to offer to co-build the canvas

The user is planning the year. They're describing conflict across functions about priorities. They're about to write a 40-page marketing plan. They're in a stage inflection (single-product to suite, regional to international). In any of those, offer to build a PGTM Canvas together, starting from their context. Use `assets/pgtm-canvas-template.md` as the blank.

The worked example at `examples/pgtm-canvas-filled-saas.md` shows what a completed canvas looks like in a real context.
