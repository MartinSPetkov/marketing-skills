# PMM Career and Company Stage

Chapters 28, 29, 30. Two axes combined: where is the PMM in their own career, and where is the company in its stage. Each combination has a different expectation.

## Career guide (Ch 28)

Three bands plus the VP jump.

### Early career (1 to 5 years)

Broad, rapid learning. Interpret market signals and execute specific functional tasks well.

Lauchengco:

> Don't shy away from detailed and concrete feedback.

Lead initiatives: website redesign, campaign strategy. Measure impact.

**Functional skills to develop.** Interpret customer and market research. Run market tests and customer interviews that produce usable insight. Competitive analysis. Product demonstrations. Sales tools. Website content. Thought-leadership content.

**Foundational skills to develop.** Writing (concision, storytelling). Oral (read the audience, adjust on the fly). Productive discussions with customers, sales, and product.

**How managers help them.** Provide frameworks, tools, and examples so they don't recreate the wheel. Don't throw them into problem-solving from scratch when a pattern already exists.

**Ready for next level when.** They've successfully managed someone (interns count). Or they've visibly moved the business forward, not just produced marketing internal work.

### Mid-level (5 to 12 years)

Director-level. Visible leaders. Lead major launches. Handle multiple product lines or product-to-solution transitions.

**Additional functional.** Deeper brand, comms, digital, and demand-gen knowledge. Enabling partners. Marketing to verticals.

**Additional foundational.** Lead cross-functional campaigns. Command respect of product and sales peers. Hire well. Management and scaling through others.

Lauchengco:

> Don't hesitate encouraging movement to lead other functions at this stage, like product management or a function in marketing.

Mid-level PMM is often where the best talent should rotate. A PMM who goes into product management and comes back is the strongest kind of senior PMM you can build.

### Senior (10+ years)

Versatile, not just familiar-playbook. Seen many launches and maintenance phases. Set up processes. Act as swiftly as the market demands.

**Has experienced failure and can talk about it.**

> If they can't discuss failure openly, it means they haven't stretched or lack personal insight.

**Additional functional.** Company spokesperson. Great at negotiating challenges and conflicts cross-functionally.

**Additional foundational.** Leads together with other functions. Seen as a leader by other functions, not just within PMM.

### The Director-to-VP jump

Lauchengco uses Ben Horowitz's "not quite ready" story. Four shifts that have to happen in the jump.

**Stop focusing on marketing excellence.** It's about your team's ability to perform and your ability to enable it.

> A VP must create connective tissue between people and functions.

**The company > the team.** The "we" needs to be cross-functional. Forge partnerships with sales and product. A VP who defends their team at the cost of the company is a Director in a VP role.

**Redirect to "why" versus "what."** Marketing teams over-report MQLs and campaign results. Great marketing leaders spend at least as much time helping the organization understand the why.

**Don't be an expert. Be open.**

> Experts feel closed. Leaders feel open.

The VP who is the most domain-expert in the room often becomes the bottleneck. The VP who creates space for others to be domain-experts amplifies the whole team.

**The hardest part.** Getting quality feedback. Ask for brutally honest feedback from people who want you to succeed. Most peers won't volunteer it.

## PMM by company stage (Ch 29)

### Early stage: Ignition

Massive learning. GTM fit gets discovered. Frequent iteration. "Hand-to-hand combat." Everything urgent and important.

Lauchengco:

> Companies at this stage aren't ready for bigger marketing budgets because they don't yet know who their best customers are.

PMM at this stage is the PGTM strategist. Interpret market signal. Translate to GTM action. Frequent back-and-forth with anyone talking to customers (design, product, sales, CS, customers themselves).

B2B signal at this stage: sales playbook taking shape. Messaging refined by response. Best evangelists being discovered.

> It's also important to lead with a point of view as much as what's in your product.

Requires director-level PMM. Ignition-stage PMM is not an entry-level job.

Graduates to next stage when GTM feels repeatable for 2-3 quarters and confidence on winning segments is higher.

### Growth stage: Rapid Rise

Fuel-on-fire. Success is mostly repeatable. But:

> What got you here isn't necessarily what will keep you growing.

PMM at this stage is the enabler of business strategy, especially new growth opportunities.

Competitive forces show up. Bigger companies adding in your space. Upstarts doing something different cheaper. PMM helps own a position via elevated story and steady cadence. Expand category notion. Actively cultivate evangelism: influencers, analysts, pundits, raving fans.

Product expansion: PMM ensures each addition matters. Don't let the portfolio bloat without positioning each new thing.

> Beware of stories getting too complex or too far ahead of what the market can bear.

New GTM models may be added. Adding PLG alongside direct sales. Adding partnerships. Each new model is a project, not a side gig.

Emerges after many quarters of strong continued growth matching stage and size expectations.

### Mature: Peak Burn

Challenge: maintain sustained growth.

> Ironically, a mature technology company can have immature product marketing.

Everyone doing their own variation on a PMM theme. No standard bar. No shared playbook. PMM impact amplified: either game-changing or debilitating.

Work is not evenly distributed. Older products may need technical upgrades but little marketing. New products need more PMM. Growth suites need a lot.

A mature competitor scenario from the book:

**Sales reports:** Competitor execs had relationships before sales showed up. Competitor invited product bake-off on their strengths. Competitor's product approach felt newer. Mature company had stale preconceptions.

**PMM response:**
- Sales qualification needed improvement. Revisit segments and qualifying criteria with sales.
- Positioning frame before features. Build a stronger positioning story.
- Advantages weren't memorable. Produce more customer success stories, more data, more memorable graphics and collateral.
- Competitive repositioning tools. Battle cards with "kill points" so sales sound smart at a moment's notice. Work with product to beat competitor in key areas.

Note the breadth. A mature company losing deals needs work on qualification, positioning, evidence, and tools simultaneously. Fixing one without the others doesn't move the win rate.

## Mature company inflection points (Ch 30)

Lauchengco opens Ch 30 with four scenarios:

- 90-year-old insurance company treating customer data as a product for others.
- 20-year-old software company with dozens of products shifting to integrated suites.
- 15-year-old company launching an Amazon Prime-like subscription.
- 10-year-old company with an English-language product expanding internationally.

Four common inflections PMM has to lead through.

### Traditional to tech-first

The construction equipment company, 100+ years old, with more software engineers than mechanical engineers. Equipment as mobile sensing unit streaming to cloud. They reorganized product into empowered teams, then rethought PMM.

> The first "customers" were internal ones: marketing, process, dealer, and field sales teams.

Created proof-of-superiority artifacts. A time study using sensor guidance vs. without.

> For early adopters, specifics matter. Vision and brand aren't enough.

The team's Valentine's Day short video was loved by tech-forward followers but not by corporate marketing. Lauchengco notes this tension is "natural and necessary." A traditional company becoming tech-first will have internal friction about voice and style. Don't paper over it.

### Single-product to multi-product

> Resist the temptation to fall into mo' product, mo' better mode.

Establish a story that frames the why before diving into product specifics.

> In the best case, people focus on the value of a solution, not which product does what.

Don't assume the same GTM motion works for the new product. New competitors, buying centers, market dynamics. Especially true for acquired products: the acquirer's motion rarely fits.

### Product to solution, service, or customer-centric

Microsoft Office old: Standard or Pro editions by which products were included. Microsoft 365 today: by customer segment (home or business). Products and cloud services move fluidly across offerings.

> Customers subscribe to the service for its total value, not for any individual product.

Multi-year enterprise licenses help new Microsoft products grow because they're already paid for, so customers use them. Same mechanic works for any service company that can bundle growth products inside a renewable contract.

### International expansion

Demandbase anchor: The US market had moved past ABM messaging. The European market still relied on it.

> Markets in different geographies are distinct.

Needs in-country field and marketing roles. PMMs focused on international tend to be overlays across multiple products or services.

Translate non-obvious challenges. A feature name that doesn't translate. Technology accessibility differences. Product behavior that doesn't culturally translate.

Don't translate the deck and call it international expansion. The playbook has to be rebuilt for the region.

## Using this reference

When advising a PMM on career moves, diagnose where they are on both axes. A senior PMM at an early-stage company needs different advice than a senior PMM at a mature company. An early-career PMM at a mature company might be starving for the kind of learning that a year at an early-stage company would force.

When advising a company on PMM hiring or structure, diagnose which stage they're actually in. Many companies describe themselves as growth-stage while their GTM is still being discovered. That's ignition, and it needs a director-level PMM, not an entry-level one.
