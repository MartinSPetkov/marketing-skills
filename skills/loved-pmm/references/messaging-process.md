# Messaging Discovery Process

Chapters 5, 21, and 22 together. The book's most contrarian material, because most PMM teams are trained on positioning-statement formulas that Lauchengco actively argues against.

## The core complaint

> Formulas are input, not output.

The popularized positioning-statement generator (the "For [target customer] who [statement of need], [product name] is a [category] that [key benefit]; unlike [primary competitive alternative], our product [statement of primary differentiation]" template) became "the straight jacket of positioning." Teams took its output and applied it as published copy. The output is derivative, jargon-filled, and focused on what the company wants to say instead of what customers need to hear.

Use the formula as a thinking tool in the private working doc. Never use its raw output as published copy.

## Positioning vs messaging

One more time, because teams collapse these.

- **Positioning** is the place your product holds in the minds of customers. How they know what you do and how you differ from alternatives. The long game.
- **Messaging** is the key things you say to reinforce your positioning, to make the company credible enough that people want to learn more. The short game.

A company can have great messaging and no positioning, and it will churn as soon as messaging shifts. A company can have crisp positioning and weak messaging, and sales will struggle to activate the position. Both matter. The distinction matters more.

## Positioning = Your Actions + Others'

70% of purchase decision-making happens outside company control. Comparison sites, reviews, ratings, social posts, forums, third-party content, employee buzz on LinkedIn. Your website and sales deck are 30% of the job.

Practical implication: positioning is not a document you write. It's the result of what you do (including product decisions, customer success, pricing, partnerships) plus what others say about you. PMM influences both, but controls neither.

When a user asks "can you write our positioning statement?" the honest first answer is: positioning is a process, not a statement. Then walk them through the messaging discovery below, which is how you earn the statement.

## Looker vs RJMetrics

The contrast from Ch 5, because it's the clearest example of what "formula output" costs.

**RJMetrics (the loser in this story):** "the best tool for running a data-driven online business. Data-driven decisions lead to better results."

Generic. Could be Excel. Lauchengco's read: "Does that help a data analyst understand why they should pay attention to this product?" No.

**Looker (the winner):** "re-invents business intelligence. Our modern data discovery platform takes a markedly different approach to analytics. Because it operates in-database, all your data is inherently drillable and explorable."

Specific. Concrete about what's different. Names the mechanism (in-database), names what the mechanism produces for the buyer (drillable and explorable).

RJMetrics had a four-year head start. Looker was acquired by Google for $2.6B after seven years. The messaging alone doesn't explain the outcome, but the difference in messaging clarity tracks the difference in category outcomes.

## The AppOmni anchor

Brendan O'Connor at an RSA cocktail party. Former Salesforce Chief Trust Officer, ServiceNow Security CTO. Running through positioning variations at a bar. Every version got the same response: "Is it similar to what [company X] is doing?"

Lauchengco's lesson: "People need to start with what's familiar to understand what's new." Your messaging is not building a new mental map from scratch. It's bridging a gap in an existing one. Find the gaps and bridge them. Don't try to replace the whole map.

## The CAST filter

Apply this to any message before shipping.

- **Clear.** Is what you do clear, and is there a reason to be curious? Is being comprehensive getting in the way of clarity?
- **Authentic.** Is the language evocative and meaningful to your customer? Will they feel known?
- **Simple.** Is it easy to understand what's compelling or different? Will customers know what's better?
- **Tested.** Has it been tested and iterated in the context customers will experience it? (Website, email, ad, sales deck.) Not tested in a Google Doc.

CAST is a private filter, not a published framework. If you're publishing "our messaging is clear, authentic, simple, and tested" you've missed the point.

## Listening questions for messaging discovery

Chapter 22. Before you write anything, run some of these with five to ten customers or prospects.

- Tell me what your average day looks like.
- What really frustrates you in how you do your job? What keeps you up at night?
- What is the straw that broke the camel's back? What made you seek a new way of solving the problem?
- If you had a magic wand to create something that could do anything, what would it do?
- What's the last problem at work you spent money to fix?

Capture their exact words. Don't paraphrase. The phrases that come back repeatedly are the phrases that belong in the messaging. If three people describe the pain as "buried in paperwork," that phrase is gold. Don't translate it into "administrative burden."

## Active listening discipline

> If you lead with your ideas, you've primed the response.

Lead with open-ended questions. Use calibration tools to make soft answers concrete: ratings, rankings, "would you hit the like button?". Capture verbatim. When you play back what you heard, play back their phrases, not yours.

## Expensify vs Concur (Ch 22, Table 22.1)

A side-by-side of authentic customer-POV messaging vs. product-name-led messaging. See `examples/expensify-vs-concur-messaging.md` for the full treatment.

The short version:

**Expensify** leads with "Sit back and let Expensify do the work." Talks about "road warriors with pockets full of receipts" and "accountants buried in paperwork." Names multiple audiences. Uses concrete modifiers: "one-click," "next-day."

**Concur** leads with "Better travel and expense management. Always." Talks about "helping businesses with their top travel and expense challenges." Leads with product names (Concur Expense, Concur Travel, Concur Invoice). Uses "automate and integrate your AP process" where Expensify uses "accounting sync."

Both say similar things about similar features. Only one sounds like it was written by someone who had a conversation with a customer.

## Workshop favorite

> Expense reports that don't suck! Hassle-free expense reporting built for employees and loved by admins.

Zoom later copied the structure: "Video conferencing that doesn't suck." The construction works because it refuses corporate voice and names a shared irritation. Use with care, but note the mechanic: a small breach of decorum creates a feeling of candor.

## Test in context

> When messaging is tested in a web page or email, not only do you get better customer input, it's easier to see unnecessary or confusing phrasing.

Don't test in a Google Doc. Build the real medium (a landing-page mock, an email preview, a sales slide) and test in that. Words that read fine in a doc read as jargon in a live design.

## Leveraging sales for messaging

A test Lauchengco recommends more than once. CEO, marketing head, and sales head each author a home-page message. Test one week each. In her case: sales head's version beat CEO's by over 1000% and beat marketing's handily. The others relied on jargon and felt less authentic. Sales hears the language customers actually use. Use that.

This doesn't mean sales writes your website. It means sales gets an equal seat at the messaging table, and their drafts get tested alongside the others.

## Leveraging search

Ch 24 is mostly category strategy, but tucks in a useful messaging move.

- Google Search Trends for relative word usage. If one of your candidate phrases is dying on the search curve, don't anchor a message to it.
- Search Journey Testing to see the path a user takes.
- Targeted ad buys testing vastly different directions (aspirational, product-led, problem-led).
- Keyword audits to see where traffic actually lands versus where you want it to land.

## Messaging takes time

Microsoft Office's shift from "desktop productivity application" replacement to "integrated office suite" took two years before sales inflected. Lauchengco's reflection: "The dogged positioning investment we made set us up to accelerate more quickly through the fat parts of the bell curve."

Don't measure messaging on a six-week campaign window. Measure it on a year or more.

## The better process, end to end

1. Listen. Five to ten conversations with customers and prospects. Capture verbatim.
2. Find the phrases that recur. These become the authentic raw material.
3. Sketch starter messaging and two to four support pillars (see `references/messaging-canvas.md`).
4. Apply CAST as a private filter. Rewrite anything that fails.
5. Build the real medium (landing page, email, slide).
6. Test with customers in the medium. Include a CEO/marketing/sales test variant if the stakes warrant it.
7. Refine. Ship. Measure over quarters, not weeks.

That's the whole process. The formula is nowhere in it.

## Engineering precision

Ch 21. A note for PMMs working with engineering-heavy products and engineers who read the messaging and flinch at oversimplification.

> Talking about something in a way that simplifies, and leaves some details out, is not being inaccurate. It's guiding toward shared understanding.

Messaging's job is to connect first before diving into details. An engineer who insists the messaging include every caveat is fighting for accuracy at the cost of comprehension. A PMM's job is to keep the message accurate enough to be true and simple enough to land, then trust the docs and the demo to carry the detail.
