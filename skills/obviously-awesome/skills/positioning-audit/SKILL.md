# Positioning audit

This skill reviews existing positioning to determine whether it still reflects reality -- competitive, market, and customer reality -- and identifies what needs to change.

## When positioning breaks down

Positioning is not permanent. It was accurate when it was written, based on the market conditions and customer knowledge at the time. Several things can make it wrong:

**Competitive changes.** A new competitor enters with a different approach. An existing competitor copies your differentiators, making your unique attributes less unique. The market leader shifts strategy and now competes in your subsegment. A well-funded startup is redefining how buyers think about the category.

**Customer changes.** Your best-fit customer profile has shifted as you've grown. The buyers who showed up early were a different type than who's buying now. Customer priorities have changed -- the problems they care most about in 2024 are different from 2020.

**Technology or regulatory changes.** A technology shift changes what's possible or what customers expect. A regulatory change creates new requirements that alter buyer priorities. Infrastructure changes reduce or remove technical barriers that previously defined competitive alternatives.

**Internal changes.** The product has evolved past the original positioning. Features added over two years have created new capabilities that weren't in the original value themes. A pivot or acquisition has changed what the company actually sells.

**Drift.** Positioning erodes through use. Marketing writes copy that modifies the message slightly. Sales reps adapt the pitch to fit individual deals. Each version is a little further from the source until no one can articulate the original position clearly.

Any of these can make positioning obsolete. The audit determines which, if any, has happened.

## Audit process

Run the audit in four phases.

---

### Phase 1: Reconstruct the current positioning

Before evaluating whether positioning is right, establish what it actually says. This sounds obvious; it is often the hardest part.

Ask the team to provide the most recent positioning canvas or equivalent document. If none exists, ask for the sales deck, the website homepage copy, and the messaging document most reps use. Reconstruct the implied positioning from these sources.

Warning signs at this stage:
- No canonical document exists; different people describe the positioning differently
- The sales deck and website tell different stories
- The positioning was documented but hasn't been reviewed in more than 18 months
- The team describes their positioning in feature terms, not value terms

If the implied positioning cannot be reconstructed clearly enough to evaluate, the audit has already found something: the first problem is that the positioning was never properly documented or has been lost to drift.

---

### Phase 2: Test each component against current reality

For each of the five positioning components, run a specific validation test.

**Competitive alternatives**

Ask: do customers still solve this problem the way they did when the positioning was written?

Specific questions to answer:
- What does the sales team hear when prospects say "we already have something for this"? Does that match the alternatives in the canvas?
- Has a new vendor entered the market in the last 12-18 months that is showing up in deals?
- Has the incumbent competitor changed its pricing, packaging, or go-to-market in a way that affects which deals you compete in?
- Are deals being lost to "do nothing" at a higher rate? That is a sign the primary alternative has effectively gotten better.

Red flags: competitors appearing in deals that don't appear in the canvas; the "primary alternative" in the canvas never coming up in sales calls; customers referencing a new category or solution type you haven't heard of.

**Unique attributes**

Ask: are the attributes in the canvas still differentiated against current alternatives?

Specific questions:
- Has any competitor launched a feature that narrows or closes the gap on your listed attributes?
- Have any attributes become table stakes in the category (something every vendor now offers)?
- Has the product shipped features in the last year that aren't reflected in the attribute list?

Red flags: prospects no longer asking about the attributes you list; sales reps supplementing the story with product capabilities not in the canvas; competitors' G2 or Capterra profiles showing attributes that used to be exclusive to you.

**Value themes**

Ask: are the value themes still what best-fit customers care most about?

Specific questions:
- Do the themes match what customers say in discovery calls right now?
- Are the proof points still current? Customers from 2021 citing 2019 metrics are not convincing in 2024.
- Have customers' priorities shifted? A team that cared most about speed in 2021 may care most about cost in 2024.

Red flags: sales reps ad-libbing new value themes in calls that aren't in the canvas; win/loss analysis showing deals turning on criteria not covered in the current positioning; proof points more than two years old with nothing newer.

**Who cares a lot**

Ask: does the "who cares a lot" description still match the customer profile that buys and succeeds most?

Specific questions:
- Pull the last 20 closed-won deals. Do they match the customer profile in the canvas?
- Pull the last 20 churned customers or closed-lost deals. Were those customers outside the "who cares a lot" profile?
- Has the company made a deliberate decision to move upmarket, downmarket, or into a new segment -- and if so, has the positioning been updated to reflect that?

Red flags: a wide spread in customer types among recent wins; customer success saying "our best customers are very different from our average customer"; SDRs generating leads from a profile that doesn't match the canvas.

**Market category**

Ask: is the category frame still working in sales conversations?

Specific questions:
- When prospects hear the category name, do they understand immediately what problem is being solved? Or do they ask clarifying questions?
- Has a new category name emerged in the market that customers use more readily?
- Has a competitor established a category frame that is now the default reference point?

Red flags: reps spending significant time explaining what the product is before they can discuss why it matters; analysts or press using a different category name than the one in the canvas; the company being compared to vendors it doesn't consider competitors.

---

### Phase 3: Score the positioning

After testing each component, score the overall positioning on three dimensions:

**Accuracy** -- does the positioning reflect what the product actually does and who it actually serves today?
- High: the canvas matches current product capabilities and current best-fit customers
- Medium: the canvas is mostly accurate but missing recent product evolution or customer profile shifts
- Low: significant capabilities or customer segments are absent from the canvas

**Differentiation** -- are the claimed advantages still genuine advantages?
- High: unique attributes are still differentiated against current alternatives; no competitor has matched them
- Medium: some attributes remain differentiated but one or two have become table stakes or been copied
- Low: most unique attributes are now available elsewhere; the positioning relies on claims customers no longer believe

**Resonance** -- does the story land in sales conversations?
- High: reps use the positioning consistently and it generates genuine prospect engagement
- Medium: reps use parts of it but supplement with other angles not in the canvas
- Low: reps have largely abandoned the official story and are winging first calls

---

### Phase 4: Recommend an action

Based on the scores, recommend one of three paths:

**Refresh (one or two components need updating)**

The positioning is largely sound but specific elements have drifted or new information needs to be incorporated. This might mean updating proof points, adding a new attribute that the product has developed, or revising the "who cares a lot" column to reflect the current best-fit profile. A refresh does not require running the full positioning workshop.

Typical triggers: Resonance is high, Accuracy is medium, Differentiation is high-to-medium. The story is working but some components are stale.

**Targeted re-work (one major component has broken down)**

One component is significantly wrong, which is destabilizing the rest. Usually this is competitive alternatives (a new major competitor has changed the landscape) or market category (the frame no longer works in conversations). This requires focused work on the broken component and then propagating the changes through the affected downstream components. Usually takes one or two working sessions.

Typical triggers: Differentiation is low (unique attributes are no longer unique); or Resonance is low but Accuracy is medium-to-high (the story is accurate but not landing, which usually means the category frame or competitive narrative is off).

**Full re-positioning**

The positioning was built for a different product, a different market, or a different customer profile. Updating components piece by piece will not fix the underlying problem; the whole foundation has shifted. This requires running the full 10-step process.

Typical triggers: Accuracy is low (the product has changed significantly); or a major market shift has made the entire competitive context obsolete; or the company has expanded far beyond its original best-fit customer profile.

---

## What the audit does not do

The audit diagnoses whether and where positioning has broken down. It does not rebuild the positioning. If the recommendation is a targeted re-work or full re-positioning, route to the appropriate skill:
- Targeted re-work on competitive alternatives: `competitive-alternatives`
- Targeted re-work on value mapping: `unique-value-mapping`
- Targeted re-work on market category: `market-frame`
- Full re-positioning: `positioning-workshop`

---

## Output format

Deliver:

1. Reconstructed current positioning, with a note on how it was derived (from canvas, from sales deck, from website copy, or from multiple inconsistent sources)
2. Component-by-component validation, with specific findings for each of the five components
3. Three-dimension score (Accuracy / Differentiation / Resonance) with brief rationale for each
4. Recommended action (Refresh / Targeted re-work / Full re-positioning) with specific next steps
5. If a refresh is recommended: the specific edits to make and where they go in the canvas
6. If a targeted re-work is recommended: which component to focus on first and which sub-skill to use
7. If full re-positioning is recommended: a brief note on what inputs to gather before starting the workshop

## Common errors in positioning audits

**Auditing the website instead of the canvas.** Website copy is already one step removed from positioning. If the audit treats the homepage headline as the positioning, it is evaluating a derivative. The positioning canvas is the source of truth; website copy should match it, not define it.

**Confusing execution problems with positioning problems.** Low win rates can come from bad positioning or from bad sales execution. If the positioning audit shows strong Accuracy and Differentiation but low Resonance, the issue may be that reps are not using the positioning rather than that the positioning is wrong. Audit the execution before recommending a re-positioning.

**Updating positioning to describe current customers instead of best-fit customers.** If 80% of the customer base has drifted away from the original "who cares a lot" profile, that does not mean the canvas should be updated to describe those customers. It may mean positioning drift has attracted the wrong customers and the "who cares a lot" should be re-sharpened to the original (or intentionally updated if a deliberate segment expansion has occurred).

**Treating new competitors as automatically invalidating the positioning.** A new competitor exists. The question is whether it changes the competitive alternative that best-fit customers consider. If the new competitor is targeting a different buyer or a different problem, it does not affect the positioning. If it is winning deals that the canvas assumes should be winning, it does.
