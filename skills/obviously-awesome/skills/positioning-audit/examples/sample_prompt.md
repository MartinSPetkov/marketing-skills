# Sample prompts -- Positioning audit

---

## 1. Auditing after a competitive shift

> We set our positioning 18 months ago and it's worked well, but the landscape has changed. Here's the canvas we built:
>
> Product: API security platform for CI/CD pipelines
> Market category: API security
> Primary competitive alternative: Manual API testing during code review (developer-run, ad hoc)
> Unique attributes: Automated scanning integrated into the CI/CD pipeline, real-time detection of authentication gaps and data exposure risks, developer-native workflow (no security team handoff required)
> Value themes: (1) Security issues caught in development, not production; (2) No change to the developer workflow -- security runs inside the pipeline they already use; (3) Full API inventory built automatically as a byproduct of scanning
> Who cares a lot: Engineering-led organizations shipping APIs at high velocity, specifically teams where developers own security outcomes and don't want to route every issue through a security team
>
> What's changed: Salt Security and Noname Security have both raised large rounds and are going after the enterprise market aggressively. We're a smaller player. Our win rates in deals above 200 engineers have dropped. Below that threshold, we're still winning consistently.
>
> Audit the positioning and tell me what to fix.

**Why this works:** A complete canvas is provided alongside specific market intelligence. The audit can test each component against what's changed -- in this case, the "who cares a lot" threshold may need tightening and the competitive alternative analysis needs to account for well-funded direct competitors that didn't exist when the canvas was built.

## 2. Auditing after significant product evolution

> Our positioning was built when we launched. Since then the product has expanded considerably and we're not sure the original canvas still describes what we actually sell.
>
> Original canvas (18 months ago):
> - Product: Factorial HR -- HR software for small businesses in Spain
> - Market category: HR software for small businesses
> - Primary competitive alternative: Excel + email + paper processes for employee management
> - Unique attributes: Spanish labor law compliance built in, digital document signing, time tracking with overtime calculation
> - Value themes: (1) Avoid compliance penalties from incorrect overtime or leave calculations; (2) Replace paper processes with digital workflows employees can use from mobile; (3) HR admin time cut by 60% for companies under 50 employees
> - Who cares a lot: Small business owners in Spain with 10-50 employees, usually without a dedicated HR person
>
> What's changed in 18 months:
> - We've expanded to cover France, Germany, Italy, and the UK -- each with their own labor law modules
> - We've moved upmarket; most new customers are 50-200 employees and many have a dedicated HR manager
> - We now offer payroll, benefits management, and an ATS module
> - Competitors like BambooHR and Personio are now in deals regularly -- we rarely compete against Excel anymore
>
> Do we need to reposition?

**Why this works:** The user is asking the right question ("do we need to reposition?") but doesn't have a clear answer yet. The audit will score each component against current reality, find that Competitive Alternatives and Who Cares A Lot have both shifted substantially, and recommend a targeted re-work that covers which components need updating without requiring a full restart.

## 3. Diagnosing drift when there is no formal canvas

> We've been in business for five years and have never formally documented our positioning. Our pitch has evolved organically and every rep tells a slightly different version. We're about to hire our first VP of Sales and need to get the story straight before they start.
>
> Here's what I can reconstruct from our most recent sales deck and website:
> - We sell data pipeline tooling for analytics teams
> - Our homepage says "the modern data stack, built for teams that move fast"
> - Our sales deck leads with a slide about "the data engineering talent shortage"
> - Our three feature categories in the deck are: pre-built connectors, automated transformation, and observability
> - Our case studies feature Spotify, Loom, and a fintech startup I can't name publicly
>
> Win rate is about 35%. We're often told we lose to Fivetran and dbt.

**Why this works:** There is no canvas, so the audit cannot immediately score components. The skill will reconstruct the implied positioning from the provided materials, identify the gaps (no explicit value themes, no "who cares a lot" definition, a category claim that doesn't differentiate), and recommend rebuilding the canvas before the VP hire rather than just trying to "fix the deck."

## Tips

**Bring the canvas, not the deck.** The audit tests whether positioning holds up. If you share a sales deck instead of the canvas, the audit is evaluating execution materials, not the underlying positioning. If you don't have a canvas, say so -- the audit will help you reconstruct one as a first step.

**Tell us what's changed, specifically.** "Things have changed" is not an audit input. The most useful inputs are: which competitors are showing up in deals that didn't used to, what prospects say in discovery that they didn't used to say, which customer segments are churning at higher rates, and which product capabilities were added in the last 12-18 months. The more specific the change intelligence, the more specific the audit findings.

**Win/loss data is the best input you can give.** If you have even informal notes from 10-15 closed-lost deals, include them. Loss reasons, stated or inferred, map directly to the component-by-component validation in the audit. A stack of "lost to price" losses is different from "lost because prospect didn't understand how we differed from X."

**Don't audit when you actually need to execute.** If the positioning is sound but the sales team isn't using it, that's a sales enablement problem, not a positioning problem. The audit will tell you this if it's the case -- but going in, be honest about whether the story works when it's told well or whether it doesn't work even when told correctly.
