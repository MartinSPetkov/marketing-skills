# Sample Prompts — Unique Value Mapping

---

## 1. Full value mapping from a feature list

> Our competitive alternatives are: manual delivery tracking via spreadsheets (primary), and FedEx/UPS enterprise software (secondary). Our product is a last-mile delivery optimization platform. Unique attributes vs. those alternatives:
> - Dynamic route optimization that updates in real-time as conditions change
> - Driver app with offline mode (works without cell signal)
> - Customer notification system with live tracking links
> - Integrates with our clients' existing WMS software
> - Built specifically for regional carriers (not designed for FedEx scale)
>
> Map these to value themes and tell me which types of customers will care most.

**Why this works:** Provides competitive alternatives upfront, lists specific attributes in concrete terms, and gives enough product context to do a real translation.

---

## 2. Focused value mapping for a single attribute

> We have one feature that seems to matter more than anything else: our database can return query results from 10 billion row datasets in under 5 seconds. The primary competitive alternative is other data warehouses that take 30–60 minutes for the same query. What is the real customer value of this, and who cares most about it?

**Why this works:** Narrow, specific, and anchored to a comparison baseline. The skill can do a deep three-level translation on this single attribute.

---

## 3. Identifying who cares most from a broad audience

> We have a product that helps field service teams manage scheduling, dispatch, and work orders. Our happy customers include HVAC companies, plumbing contractors, electrical contractors, pest control companies, and lawn care companies. We have 3 value themes: faster scheduling, real-time job tracking, and automatic customer communication. Which of these customer types cares most about each theme, and how should we prioritize?

**Why this works:** Good setup for Step 7 analysis. The skill will map value themes to customer segment characteristics and help identify the highest-priority subsegment.

---

## Tips

**Include the competitive alternatives before listing attributes.** The skill cannot assess what is "unique" without knowing the baseline. Always specify: "Unique attributes vs. [primary alternative]."

**List everything, even negatives.** Mandatory professional services for onboarding sounds like a weakness but can be a genuine attribute for customers who want high-touch implementation. List it and let the skill assess its value.

**Ground value claims in customer language.** "Saves time" is weak. "Our customers say they go from 3 hours of reporting every Friday afternoon to 20 minutes" is strong. Share quotes, metrics, or even rough estimates — anything more specific than generic benefit language.

**Push for proof.** The skill will flag unproven claims. That is a feature, not a bug. Unproven value claims are unusable in sales and marketing. Use the flagging to build a list of things you need to validate with customers.
