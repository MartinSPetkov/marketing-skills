# Sales Story Builder

This skill translates a completed positioning canvas into a structured sales narrative — the story a salesperson tells from the opening of a first call through to the close.

## Why a sales story, not just messaging

After completing positioning, the most natural impulse is to update the website. But website visitors read asynchronously and can navigate at their own pace. Sales calls are linear: the salesperson has one shot to create context before the prospect forms their own (often wrong) interpretation.

The sales story is the positioning in motion. It tells prospects not just what the product is, but why the problem matters, why current alternatives fall short, what an ideal solution would look like, and then why this product is that solution. Done well, it gets prospects to the "aha moment" — the point where they understand what the product is and why they need it — in the first five minutes of a call.

Without an agreed sales story, every rep tells a different version of the pitch. Positioning erodes. Close rates stay low not because the product is bad but because prospects never get the full picture in the right order.

## Prerequisites

A completed positioning canvas (or at minimum: competitive alternatives, value themes, market category, and who cares a lot). If the canvas is not ready, use the `positioning-canvas` skill first.

## The six-part sales story arc

Build the narrative in this order. Each part sets up the next.

---

### Part 1: Problem context

**What it does:** Establishes the business situation that makes the problem real and urgent. Gives the prospect language to understand the domain and signals that you understand their world.

**How to write it:** One to three sentences. State the business context your customers operate in. Focus on the world from the buyer's perspective, not from the product's perspective.

Bad: "Our company was founded to solve the problem of data silos."
Good: "Insurance companies today are under pressure to process claims faster — customers expect digital, self-serve claims submission, but most claims processes still require phone calls, manual forms, and callbacks."

The problem context should be true regardless of whether your product exists. It describes the world your buyers live in.

---

### Part 2: How customers solve it today (and where that falls short)

**What it does:** Describes the primary competitive alternative and its limitations. This is the most important part of the story to get right. It defines what "better" means.

**How to write it:** State the most common way customers handle this problem today. Then describe specifically where that approach fails — not in vague terms ("it's slow") but in terms of what customers cannot do because of those limitations.

Key: the limitations you name should map directly to the value your product delivers. If you're going to say your product enables real-time decision-making, the setup should name that customers currently cannot make decisions in real time with their current approach.

Example: "Most companies handle this in Excel. Analysts spend 3–4 hours every Friday afternoon pulling data from multiple systems, cleaning it, and building the weekly report. By the time it's ready, the data is already 48 hours old. Any decision that depends on it is working from yesterday's picture."

Do not mention your product yet.

---

### Part 3: The perfect world

**What it does:** Describes what a perfect solution would look like before naming your product as that solution. This lets the prospect agree with the criteria before you introduce the product.

**How to write it:** Describe the features of an ideal solution, given what you just said about the problem and its current inadequacy. Use language that maps to your value themes.

Example: "In a perfect world, analysts wouldn't have to build reports manually. The data would update continuously so every decision-maker would always have a current picture. And the insights would be pushed to the people who need them, rather than requiring analysts to custom-pull every report."

By the time you introduce the product, the prospect has mentally agreed that something with these qualities would be valuable — because you described it in terms of solving their problem, not in terms of your features.

---

### Part 4: Product introduction and market category

**What it does:** Introduces the product by placing it in the chosen market category. This is the moment to use the market frame.

**How to write it:** One or two sentences. Name the market category first, then the product. The market category does the contextual framing; the product is what occupies that frame.

Example: "Acme Analytics is a real-time business intelligence platform for mid-market manufacturing companies."

Do not lead with features. The market category frames what the product is. Features come next.

---

### Part 5: Value themes with proof

**What it does:** Walks through each value theme from the canvas, explaining specifically what the product enables and providing proof.

**How to write it:** One section per value theme. For each: name the theme, describe the capability that enables it, and provide at least one piece of evidence (customer metric, third-party quote, or demonstrated result).

This is not a feature list. Each theme is a story of what the customer can do — with a concrete example of them actually doing it.

Example:
"The first thing our customers notice is that they can stop building reports. Within the first week of deployment, analysts reclaim an average of 4 hours per week they were spending on manual reporting. [Customer name] told us they freed up half an FTE in their analytics team in the first month.

The second thing they notice is decisions getting faster. Because the data is always current, teams stop waiting for the Friday report to answer questions that come up on Tuesday. [Customer name] reduced their monthly close cycle from 18 days to 9 days in the first quarter."

---

### Part 6: Next step

**What it does:** Closes the narrative with a clear, specific action for the prospect to take.

**How to write it:** One sentence. Make it concrete and low-friction.

This is not a call to action on the website. This is the end of a live conversation. The next step should match where this prospect is in their buying process:
- For a first call: "I'd love to show you how this works in an environment that looks like yours. Can we schedule a 30-minute demo for next week?"
- For a demo follow-up: "Based on what you saw today, does it make sense to involve your IT team in the next conversation?"
- For late-stage: "Can we set up a call with your finance team to walk through the ROI model?"

## Additional components for a complete sales deck

The six-part arc covers the narrative. A complete sales deck also needs:

- **Objection handlers** — for the 3–5 most common objections. Write them as pre-emptive statements, not as reactive responses. Ideally weave them into the story rather than including a separate "objection handling" slide.
- **Customer proof** — a brief case study or customer logo slide with one specific outcome. Best placed after Part 5.
- **"Why now" framing** — if a trend was identified in Step 9, include a brief statement about what is changing in the market that makes this product more urgent now than 12 months ago.
- **Common objection: "Why not the market leader?"** — If positioning is Big Fish Small Pond, include a specific slide or talking point that addresses how you compare to the incumbent on the criteria that matter to this subsegment.

---

## Output format

Deliver:

1. **Complete six-part sales story** — written in pitch language, not outline language. Each part should be a paragraph or two that a salesperson could actually say.
2. **Sales deck structure** — slide-by-slide outline mapping the story to a presentation format
3. **Key messages per part** — one-sentence summaries for parts 2, 3, and 5 that can be used as standalone talking points
4. **Talk time guidance** — approximate time allocation per section for a 30-minute first call
5. **Objection flag** — top 2–3 likely objections that the story should be prepared to handle, with brief notes on where to address them

## Common errors in sales stories

**Starting with the company or product.** Prospects don't care about your company in the first two minutes. Start with their problem.

**Using the sales story for investor pitches.** The investor pitch describes a large, future market. The sales story describes an immediate problem and a proven solution. They are different documents.

**Generic problem statements.** "Companies struggle with data management" is not a problem context. "Mid-size manufacturers lose an average of 12 hours per week reconciling production data across three disconnected systems" is.

**Skipping the perfect world.** Without Part 3, the product introduction lands as "here is what we do" rather than "here is what you agreed you needed, and we provide it."
