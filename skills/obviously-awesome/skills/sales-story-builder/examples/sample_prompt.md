# Sample prompts -- Sales story builder

---

## 1. Building from a complete positioning canvas

> We finished our positioning workshop last week. Here's what we landed on:
>
> Product: Clearpath Robotics (OTTO Motors division) -- autonomous indoor delivery vehicles
> Market category: Autonomous industrial vehicles
> Primary competitive alternative: Manual forklift operators and fixed conveyor systems
> Unique attributes: Dynamic facility mapping and navigation (no fixed routes), fleet dispatch and management, standard vehicle controls familiar to operators
> Value themes: (1) Materials move continuously and adapt to production changes without rerouting fixed infrastructure; (2) Fleet-level visibility into delivery status across the facility; (3) Operators can run the vehicles from day one -- no robotics programming required
> Who cares a lot: Manufacturers with variable production flows, facilities doing frequent layout changes, warehouses with high labor costs for manual material movement
> Trend: Consumer autonomous vehicle adoption has made buyers familiar with the concept and reduced skepticism
>
> Build the sales story.

**Why this works:** All required components are present. The skill can write all six parts without gaps and weave the trend into both Part 1 and Part 5.

## 2. Building with the trend as a "why now" accelerator

> Here's the positioning canvas for our product:
>
> Product: Redgate Software -- database change management tools
> Market category: Database DevOps tooling (we're the incumbent leader)
> Primary competitive alternative: Manual database change processes -- scripts, tribal knowledge, manual reviews
> Value themes: (1) Automate database changes across the entire development pipeline; (2) Compliance and full audit trail for every database change; (3) Plug databases into existing DevOps workflows without rebuilding anything
> Who cares a lot: Development teams already on CI/CD who can't get databases into the pipeline, companies in regulated industries (financial services, healthcare) with change management requirements, teams that have had a production incident from an untracked change
> Trend: DevOps adoption -- development teams have standardized CI/CD for application code, but databases are consistently the last manual step in an otherwise automated pipeline
>
> Build the full sales story including the deck structure.

**Why this works:** The DevOps trend is strong enough to carry Part 1 on its own. A well-built story here opens with "every development team we talk to has automated everything except their databases" -- that one sentence frames the entire problem before the product is mentioned.

## 3. Refining an existing pitch that starts with the product

> Our sales team currently opens calls like this: "We're Acme Analytics, a real-time BI platform. We help mid-market manufacturers get better data faster. Our three main features are live dashboards, automated reporting, and drill-down analytics."
>
> We know this is wrong -- it leads with features and we're losing the first two minutes of every call. Here's our actual positioning:
>
> Competitive alternative: Weekly reports built manually in Excel by analysts
> Value themes: (1) Analysts reclaim 4+ hours per week they spend building reports; (2) Every decision runs on current data, not last week's picture; (3) Insights go to decision-makers automatically rather than requiring custom pulls on request
> Who cares a lot: Mid-market manufacturers where operations and finance teams are making time-sensitive decisions on stale data
> Market category: Real-time business intelligence for mid-market manufacturing
>
> Rewrite the pitch as a proper six-part sales story.

**Why this works:** The user already knows their current pitch is wrong and can name exactly where it fails. Providing the existing pitch alongside the positioning components gives the skill enough to show the contrast -- the before/after makes the output immediately useful for sales training.

## Tips

**Provide the competitive alternative explicitly.** The skill can write a generic "current solutions fall short" section without it, but Part 2 is the most important part of the story to get right. The limitations you name in Part 2 are what "better" means for your prospects. If you haven't defined the primary alternative, name it.

**Tell us what proof you have.** Part 5 requires at least one piece of evidence per value theme -- a customer metric, a specific outcome, a third-party reference. If you don't have proof yet, say so. The skill will write the value theme and mark the proof placeholder as [VALIDATE] so you know what to fill in before using the story in a live call.

**Specify who gets the story.** A story for an SDR's first cold call is different from one for a senior AE's demo follow-up or an exec-level QBR. If you have a specific context in mind, name it -- the CTA and talk time guidance in the output will match where you actually are in the sale.

**Don't provide a deck first.** If you share your current slide deck and ask the skill to "fix it," it will try to work around the deck structure rather than rebuild the narrative from the positioning. Give the canvas components, get the story, then map the story to slides using the deck structure in the output.
