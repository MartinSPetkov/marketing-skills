# Competitive Alternatives (Step 4)

This skill executes Step 4 of April Dunford's positioning process: identifying what your best-fit customers would actually use if your product didn't exist — from their perspective, not yours.

## Why this step matters

Most teams get competitive alternatives wrong in a predictable way: they list the startups they monitor on Twitter, the companies they lost funding rounds to, and the logos on the "compare us to" pages they've built. These are founder competitors, not customer competitors.

Customers mostly haven't heard of your startup rivals. They are trying to solve a problem using whatever is available to them today. The alternatives in their minds are the tools they actually use, the processes they currently run, and the workarounds they have built. Understanding these real alternatives is the foundation of every positioning decision that follows.

**The yardstick problem:** Your product is only "better" relative to what customers compare it to. If customers compare you to spreadsheets, "easier to use" means easier than a spreadsheet. If they compare you to an enterprise platform, "easier to use" means something completely different. Getting the comparison wrong makes the entire value proposition meaningless.

## When to push back

Refuse to produce a final competitive alternatives list if:

1. The user has not identified best-fit customers (Step 1). The alternatives must be grounded in how those specific customers think — not "customers in general." Ask them to complete Step 1 first.
2. The user only wants to list direct product competitors. Push back: status quo and "do nothing" must be considered. In most B2B markets, the most common competitive alternative is a combination of spreadsheets, manual processes, and existing general-purpose software.

## How to run this step

### Phase 1: Anchor to best-fit customers

Start by naming the best-fit customer list from Step 1. If it's not available, ask the user to describe them.

Then ask these questions, framed around those specific customers:

- "If your product disappeared tomorrow, what would those customers go back to?"
- "What were they using to handle this problem before they adopted your product?"
- "When you ask them why they chose you, what do they say they evaluated besides you?"
- "What do prospects say they are 'also looking at' in sales conversations?"
- "What do you lose deals to — and what reason do prospects give?"

Write down every answer, even if it seems obvious or embarrassing ("we lose to Excel a lot").

### Phase 2: Categorize the alternatives

Organize the raw list into 2–5 groups. Common clusters in B2B software:

- **Do it manually / status quo** — spreadsheets, documents, email threads, physical binders, hiring a person to do it
- **General-purpose software used as a workaround** — CRM used as a project tracker, Slack used as a support queue, Google Sheets used as a database
- **Adjacent category tools** — tools from a neighboring market that partially solve the problem
- **Direct category competitors** — products that describe themselves as doing what you do
- **Do nothing / ignore the problem** — especially common for newer problem spaces where buyers don't yet recognize the cost of inaction

Focus on the most common alternatives for best-fit customers, ranked by frequency. A competitor that 1 in 100 prospects mentions is not worth positioning against.

### Phase 3: Validate the perspective shift

After building the list, run this check: would a best-fit customer recognize this list as accurate? Or does it reflect what the founders think rather than what customers experience?

Common validation problems:
- The list is dominated by startup competitors that customers have never heard of
- "Do nothing" or "manual processes" is not on the list for a product in a nascent category
- The list is too long — if it has more than 5 groups, collapse it

### Phase 4: Identify the primary alternative

For most products, one alternative cluster is far more common than the others. Identify it. This is the primary competitive frame that all positioning work will be done against.

The primary alternative determines:
- What "better" means to customers (it means better than this, not better than your startup rivals)
- Which unique attributes actually matter (only the ones that are different from the primary alternative)
- The value proof you need (you have to show improvement over this baseline, not over an abstract ideal)

## Output format

Deliver:

1. **Competitive alternatives list** — 2–5 named clusters with brief descriptions
2. **Primary alternative** — the one most common for best-fit customers, flagged clearly
3. **Ranking** — ordered from most to least common
4. **Founder vs. customer gap** (if present) — call out explicitly if the team's current view of competition differs significantly from how customers see it. This gap is almost always the source of weak positioning.

Example output structure:

```
COMPETITIVE ALTERNATIVES — [Product Name]

Primary alternative: Manual processes + spreadsheets
(Most best-fit customers were previously using Excel + email chains)

Full list (ranked by frequency for best-fit customers):
1. Manual processes / spreadsheets — most common; customers used this before adopting
2. [Adjacent tool category] — some prospects evaluate this alongside you
3. [Direct competitor] — mentioned in ~20% of sales conversations
4. Do nothing — small percentage, usually early-stage companies

Note: [Startup competitor X] and [Startup competitor Y] do not appear
on this list because best-fit customers have not heard of them.
They are competitive threats for funding, not for deals.
```

## What comes next

The competitive alternatives list is the direct input to Step 5 (unique attributes). Attributes are only "unique" when compared to these specific alternatives. With a clear primary alternative identified, Step 5 produces a much tighter, more defensible list of true differentiators.
