# 10-Step Positioning Process — Reference

From *Obviously Awesome* by April Dunford (2019).

## The five (plus one) components of effective positioning

Every step in the process builds toward these six components:

| Component | What it captures |
|---|---|
| Competitive alternatives | What customers would do if the product didn't exist |
| Unique attributes | Features and capabilities the product has that alternatives lack |
| Value (and proof) | The benefit those attributes enable — in customer terms, with evidence |
| Target market characteristics | The identifiable traits of customers who care most about that value |
| Market category | The frame of reference that makes the value obvious |
| Relevant trend (bonus) | What makes this product important *right now* |

These components are interdependent and must be defined in the right order. Attributes are only "unique" relative to alternatives. Value follows from attributes. Target customers are those who most value the value themes. Market category is the frame that puts everything together. Getting the order wrong produces positioning that looks complete but doesn't connect.

## Step-by-step quick reference

| Step | Name | Key question |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Identify best-fit customers | Who loves the product and why? |
| 2 | Form a positioning team | Who needs to be in the room? |
| 3 | Align vocabulary + release baggage | Do we share definitions? What assumptions are we holding? |
| 4 | List true competitive alternatives | What would customers use if we didn't exist? |
| 5 | Isolate unique attributes | What do we have that the alternatives don't? |
| 6 | Map attributes to value themes | What can customers do because of those attributes? |
| 7 | Determine who cares a lot | Which customer characteristics predict high affinity for that value? |
| 8 | Choose a market frame + positioning style | What category makes our value obvious? Head to Head, Big Fish, or New Game? |
| 9 | Layer on a trend | Is there a current shift that makes this more urgent? |
| 10 | Capture the positioning | Canvas + document for company-wide sharing |

## The three positioning traps

**Trap 1 — Default positioning:** The product evolved but the positioning didn't. The team still describes it the way they originally imagined it, even though customers use it differently. (Dunford's own startup: built a "database" that customers actually needed as a "data warehouse.")

**Trap 2 — Stale positioning:** The product's positioning was good once, but the market shifted. A "diet muffin" becomes irrelevant when the market moves to "gluten-free paleo snacks."

**Trap 3 — Investor positioning:** Pitching to investors requires describing the future of a large market. Pitching to customers requires solving an immediate problem. These are different stories for different audiences. Never let investor positioning leak into customer-facing materials.

## Signs of weak positioning (diagnostic)

- Prospects don't understand the product quickly and require extended explanation
- Long sales cycles with low close rates
- Customers who bought for the wrong reasons and now want features you're not building
- Price pressure and constant discount requests
- High early churn as customers realize the product isn't what they thought

## The positioning styles

Detailed in the `market-frame` skill. Quick reference:

| Style | When to use | Core challenge |
|---|---|---|
| Head to Head | No strong leader exists yet, or you genuinely beat the leader on established criteria | Must win at the game as currently defined |
| Big Fish, Small Pond | Clear market leader exists, but a subsegment has unmet needs you can own | Must prove the subsegment's needs are distinct and important |
| Create a New Game | No existing category puts your strengths at the center | Must educate the market that the category needs to exist, then prove you lead it |

## The positioning canvas (Step 10 output)

```
Product name and one-line description:

Market category (and subcategory if applicable):

Competitive alternatives → Unique attributes → Value → Who cares a lot:
[Column 1]           → [Column 2]          → [Col 3] → [Column 4]

Relevant trend (if applicable):
```

## What comes after the canvas

1. **Sales story** — translate positioning into a narrative arc for first sales calls: problem definition → current solutions' gaps → perfect world → product intro → value themes → next step. Use `sales-story-builder`.
2. **Messaging document** — marketing writes messaging from the canvas, not from scratch. A master messaging document keeps all future copy anchored to the positioning.
3. **Product roadmap** — features get prioritized differently once positioning is clear. The roadmap for "accounting software" looks very different from "financial services platform."
4. **Pricing** — pricing reflects the market category. After repositioning from "database" to "data warehouse," Dunford's company raised prices — because data warehouses command higher prices than commodity databases.
5. **Positioning review** — set a calendar reminder for 6 months. Use `positioning-audit` when major competitive or market events occur.
