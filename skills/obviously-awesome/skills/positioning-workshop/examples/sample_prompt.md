# Sample Prompts — Positioning Workshop

These examples show how to get the best results from the positioning workshop skill. The more context you provide upfront, the further you can get in a single session.

---

## 1. Full workshop from scratch

> We need to reposition our product. We built a compliance automation tool for HR teams — it started as "better employee handbook management" but our happiest customers are using it to automate the entire onboarding compliance workflow, not just the handbook. They rave about cutting their compliance review time from 3 weeks to 2 days. But our website still says "modern employee handbook software" and prospects keep comparing us to handbook tools that charge $5/month. We're losing deals to that comparison even though we're doing something completely different. Can you run the full Dunford positioning process with me?
>
> Our best customers: TechCorp (500 employees, HR team of 12, high hiring volume), FinanceGroup (1,200 employees, heavy regulatory environment), StartupX (150 employees, no dedicated compliance staff).

**Why this works:** Clear description of the product, explicit description of the positioning problem (default positioning), names of best customers, and a signal from customer behavior (3 weeks → 2 days). The skill can immediately start with Step 1 and use the named customers as anchors.

---

## 2. Reposition after market shift

> We've been selling a "diet-friendly meal delivery" service for 5 years and we used to grow fast. But growth has slowed significantly in the last 18 months even though we haven't changed anything. Our customers are the same people — but the conversations feel different. They used to talk about "losing weight," now they talk about "eating clean" or "meal prepping." We wonder if our positioning is outdated. Walk me through the Dunford process to figure out if we need to reposition and what the new position should be.

**Why this works:** Classic Trap 2 scenario (market shifted, positioning didn't). Provides enough context to diagnose the issue and start the process.

---

## 3. Single-step entry (Step 4 focus)

> I want to work on Step 4 of the Dunford process — competitive alternatives. We have a product that automates invoice reconciliation for accounting teams. We're not sure if our customers see us competing with other reconciliation software, with Excel, or with their outsourced accounting firms. Can you help me figure out what they actually compare us to?

**Why this works:** Directly names the step and the uncertainty. The skill will focus on Step 4 specifically.

---

## 4. Positioning validation

> We went through our own positioning exercise last quarter and landed on: we are "CRM for investment banks." We chose this because our relationship-mapping feature is the only thing that really matters to investment bankers, and Siebel/Salesforce can't do it. Before we rewrite the website and retrain sales, can you validate whether this positioning is solid using the Dunford framework?

**Why this works:** Positioning already exists — the skill will audit it against the 10-step framework and identify any gaps or weak assumptions.

---

## Tips for best results

**Name your best-fit customers.** The whole process starts from them. Even vague descriptions ("our 5 biggest customers," "the ones who refer us most") are better than nothing. If you can share what they say about the product in their own words, do it.

**Share the current positioning.** If you already have a website headline, a sales deck intro, or a standard one-liner, share it. The skill will compare it against what emerges from the process and flag the gaps.

**Tell us what is broken.** Long sales cycles, high churn, constant price pressure, prospects comparing you to the wrong thing — the more specific the symptom, the faster the diagnosis.

**Bring customer quotes.** Verbatim language from win/loss interviews, support tickets, or sales calls is gold. The skill will use it to pressure-test value themes and market category choices.

**Come with your assumptions explicitly stated.** "We think we compete with Salesforce but we're not sure." "We've always thought of ourselves as a developer tool but our biggest users are actually product managers." Stating assumptions up front helps the skill challenge them faster.
