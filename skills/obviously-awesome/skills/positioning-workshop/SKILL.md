# Obviously Awesome: Full Positioning Workshop

This skill facilitates April Dunford's 10-step positioning process from *Obviously Awesome* (2019). It is a deliberate, structured exercise — not a messaging sprint.

Read `references/10-step-overview.md` before starting to load the full step-by-step structure.

## The core premise

Positioning is not a tagline exercise. It is the act of deliberately defining how you are the best at something that a defined market cares deeply about. Most companies have weak positioning because they never chose it — they defaulted to the market they set out to build for, even after the product evolved. This process fixes that by starting from what customers already know and love, not from what the founders imagined.

**Positioning determines:** which market you compete in, who your real competitors are, what features matter, how you price, and what story sales tells. Get it wrong and every marketing dollar and sales hour fights uphill. Get it right and everything becomes easier.

## When to push back (do not skip)

Refuse to run this workshop and explain why if:

1. The product has no paying customers or no users who clearly love it. The process requires pattern recognition from real happy customers — without them, you are guessing. Advise the user to keep positioning loose until patterns emerge.
2. Only one person is present and they are not the business or product owner. Positioning must have cross-functional buy-in or it will not be adopted. Recommend forming a team before proceeding.
3. The user wants to position for investors, not customers. Investor positioning and customer positioning are different documents with different goals. This process produces customer positioning only.

State the objection clearly. Proceed only after the user resolves it.

## Workshop flow

Work through the 10 steps in order. Each step has inputs and a clear exit condition. Do not skip steps or combine them prematurely — the order matters because each step informs the next.

---

### Step 1: Identify best-fit customers

**Goal:** Create a short reference list of the customers who love the product most.

Ask the user to describe or list:
- Customers who bought quickly and without heavy negotiation
- Customers who became advocates, gave referrals, or served as references
- Customers who use the product as intended (not bending it toward something else)
- Customers who renew without being asked and rarely request features outside the roadmap

If the user has many customers, have them sort: who are the top 5–10% who clearly love the product? These are the "best-fit customers" and they are the lens for every subsequent step.

**Exit condition:** A named or described set of best-fit customers that the team agrees represents the ideal. Move to Step 2.

---

### Step 2: Form a positioning team

**Goal:** Confirm who should be involved and who will own the output.

For a workshop to produce positioning that actually gets used, it needs:
- The business or product owner (CEO at a startup; division or BU leader at a larger company)
- At least one person from sales or customer-facing roles
- At least one person from marketing
- Optionally: product lead, customer success lead

Positioning owned only by marketing will not be adopted by sales. Positioning without the business owner's buy-in will be overridden at the first board meeting.

**Exit condition:** The user has confirmed who is in the room (or is participating in this exercise). Move to Step 3.

---

### Step 3: Align vocabulary and release positioning baggage

**Goal:** Get shared definitions and surface long-held assumptions that may be wrong.

Cover three alignment areas with the team:

**Shared definitions:** Make sure everyone means the same thing by "positioning," "competitive alternative," "unique attribute," and "value." Use examples from the product to make them concrete.

**Positioning baggage:** Ask: "When and why did we first describe this product as [current category]?" If the answer traces back to the original product vision rather than current customer reality, that is baggage. Arm & Hammer spent decades as a baking product when deodorizer was the growing market — the history locked them in. Surface this explicitly.

**Suspension of defaults:** Get explicit agreement from the team that for this exercise, the current positioning is a hypothesis to test, not a fact to protect. Any market category, any competitive frame, is on the table.

**Exit condition:** Team shares definitions and agrees to treat current positioning as provisional. Move to Step 4.

---

### Step 4: List true competitive alternatives

This step is handled in depth by the `competitive-alternatives` skill. Summarize the goal here and route if needed.

**Goal:** Identify what best-fit customers would use if this product didn't exist — from the customer's perspective, not the founder's.

Key insight: customers rarely see competitors the way founders do. They compare based on the problem they are solving, not the product category founders are competing in. A project management tool might compete with email and shared spreadsheets, not with other project management software — because that is what customers actually use today.

Ask:
- "What would your best-fit customers do if we disappeared tomorrow?"
- "What were they using before they adopted your product?"
- "What do prospects say they are evaluating besides you?"

Cluster the list into 2–5 groups. Focus on the most common alternatives for best-fit customers; ignore rare edge cases.

**Exit condition:** A ranked, clustered list of competitive alternatives as customers see them. Move to Step 5.

---

### Step 5: Isolate unique attributes

**Goal:** List all features and capabilities the product has that the competitive alternatives lack.

Be expansive at this stage. Include:
- Technical features and patents
- Business model differences (e.g., SaaS vs. installed, transaction-based vs. seat-based)
- Delivery model differences (e.g., hands-on onboarding vs. self-serve)
- Team expertise or credentials that competitors lack
- Partnerships or integrations the alternatives don't have
- Data or network effects

Do not evaluate importance yet — list everything. Items that seem negative to some team members should still be listed (a mandatory onsite implementation might be a weakness to sales but a strength to customers who want high-touch service).

Focus on "consideration attributes" — things that influence whether a customer buys — rather than "retention attributes" (like support quality) that matter more after purchase.

**Exit condition:** A complete list of unique attributes vs. the competitive alternatives. Move to Step 6.

---

### Step 6: Map attributes to value themes

This step is handled in depth by the `unique-value-mapping` skill. Summarize the goal here.

**Goal:** Translate unique attributes into value — what customers can do because of those attributes.

Use the three-level translation:
- **Feature:** something the product does or has
- **Benefit:** what the feature enables
- **Value:** how this maps to a customer goal

Example: "patented query algorithm" → "faster query results" → "analysts get answers in minutes instead of hours, letting them respond to customers during the call instead of calling back."

Group related attributes into 1–4 value themes. These themes become the pillars of everything downstream.

**Exit condition:** 1–4 named value themes with supporting attributes and provable value statements. Move to Step 7.

---

### Step 7: Determine who cares a lot

**Goal:** Define the customer characteristics that predict strong affinity for the value themes.

This is not demographic segmentation ("companies with 100–500 employees"). It is behavioral and contextual: what is it about a company or person that makes them care deeply about the value this product delivers?

Ask for each value theme:
- Who would pay a premium to have this?
- Who has tried to solve this problem and failed with current alternatives?
- Who feels the pain of not having this most acutely?

The best-fit customer list from Step 1 is the evidence base. What do those customers have in common that their less-enthusiastic counterparts do not?

Characteristics to look for: industry vertical, company growth stage, internal team structure, regulatory environment, existing tech stack, company size (by specific metric, not just headcount), buying trigger events.

**Exit condition:** A set of 3–6 identifiable customer characteristics that describe best-fit prospects. Move to Step 8.

---

### Step 8: Choose a market frame of reference and positioning style

This step is handled in depth by the `market-frame` skill. Summarize the goal here.

**Goal:** Select the market category that makes the product's value obvious to best-fit customers, and choose the right positioning style.

Three styles are available (detailed in the `market-frame` skill):
- **Head to Head:** Compete to win the whole existing market. Only viable if the product is demonstrably better than the current leader on established criteria, or if no strong leader exists yet.
- **Big Fish, Small Pond:** Dominate a subsegment of an existing market. Best for startups that cannot beat the market leader across the board but can clearly win for a specific customer type.
- **Create a New Game:** Define a new market category. Hardest and most resource-intensive — only appropriate when no existing category puts the strengths at the center.

Use abductive reasoning: what category of products typically has these features and delivers this value? Look at adjacent growing markets. Avoid asking customers directly — they will anchor to the most obvious existing category.

**Exit condition:** A chosen market frame and positioning style with rationale. Move to Step 9.

---

### Step 9: Layer on a trend (optional)

**Goal:** Identify whether a current industry trend reinforces the positioning and adds urgency.

Trends are not market categories. They are characteristics that happen to be new and important right now. A market category tells customers what the product is. A trend tells them why they should care about it today.

The three circles that must overlap: product strengths, market context, and trend. If all three intersect, use the trend. If the connection is forced, skip it — a boring position that is clear beats a trendy position that is confusing.

Ask: "Is there a technology shift, regulatory change, or customer behavior trend that makes this product more urgent or more strategic for buyers right now?"

**Exit condition:** Either a relevant trend identified and linked to the positioning, or a decision to proceed without one. Move to Step 10.

---

### Step 10: Capture positioning for company-wide sharing

This step is handled by the `positioning-canvas` skill.

**Goal:** Produce a completed positioning canvas that can be shared across marketing, sales, product, and customer success.

The canvas captures:
- Product name and one-line description
- Market category (and subcategory if applicable)
- Competitive alternatives → unique attributes → value → who cares a lot
- Relevant trend (if any)

After the canvas, route to `sales-story-builder` to translate the positioning into a sales narrative the team can use immediately.

**Exit condition:** A completed, shared positioning canvas. Positioning workshop complete.

---

## Facilitation notes

- Keep the group focused on the best-fit customer lens throughout. When team members disagree, ask: "What would our best customers say?"
- When the team gets stuck, it is usually in Steps 4 or 8. Step 4 requires customers to be the authority, not founders. Step 8 requires letting go of the original product vision.
- The whole 10-step process in a workshop setting takes 4–8 hours with a full team. Asynchronously with one person it can take 2–3 focused sessions.
- Positioning is not permanent. It should be revisited every 6 months or after a major competitive or market event. Use the `positioning-audit` skill for that.
