# Positioning Styles — Detailed Reference

From *Obviously Awesome* by April Dunford (2019).

## Quick selection guide

| Your situation | Recommended style |
|---|---|
| You are the market leader | Head to Head — protect the current definition |
| Strong leader exists, you have a clear niche | Big Fish, Small Pond |
| You are a startup entering a crowded market | Almost always Big Fish, Small Pond |
| No leader exists yet in a defined category | Head to Head — race to become the leader |
| No existing category puts your strengths center stage | Create a New Game (last resort) |
| You have deep pockets and a long timeline | Create a New Game (if product genuinely demands it) |

## Head to Head in depth

### What you are claiming

"We are the best option for most buyers in this market, measured against the criteria buyers already use to evaluate."

You are not claiming to be better for a niche. You are claiming to be better — period.

### The purchase criteria question

Head to Head only works if the established purchase criteria favor you. If you accept the current definition of the market and win, you need to win on the metrics buyers already care about. If the established criteria put you at a disadvantage, you need to either redefine them (very hard) or choose a different style.

### When a startup can use Head to Head

One scenario: an emerging category where multiple products exist but no clear leader has emerged. "Smart home security" in 2015 was like this — many products, no dominant brand. A startup could legitimately claim leadership without beating an incumbent. In this case, the startup should move fast to establish the category norms before someone else does.

Another scenario: an established leader has a structural weakness that a major technology shift exposes. Kodak was the leader in film cameras but digital photography changed the criteria. A startup entering with digital had a legitimate Head to Head opportunity because the incumbent couldn't adapt without cannibalizing its core business.

### The defense problem

If you are the incumbent defending Head to Head, every competitor is trying to redefine the purchase criteria in a way that disadvantages you. Your job is to keep the conversation on current criteria where you win, and respond quickly when a competitor introduces new criteria that customers find compelling.

## Big Fish, Small Pond in depth

### What you are claiming

"We are the best option for buyers in [specific subsegment] who have [specific unmet need] that the category leader doesn't address."

You are not claiming to be better for everyone. You are claiming to be better for this specific group.

### The subsegment definition test

A valid subsegment has three properties:

1. **Identifiable:** You can make a list of companies or people who fit the subsegment criteria without needing to survey them. "Companies with more than 1,000 employees" is identifiable via LinkedIn. "Companies that value design" is not.

2. **Needs-differentiated:** The subsegment has genuinely different requirements from the broader market, not just slightly different preferences. Investment banks need relationship modeling. Banks in Europe have different regulatory requirements than US banks. These are real differences that the market leader hasn't addressed.

3. **Large enough:** You can meet your near-term revenue goals by targeting only this subsegment. If you need 30 customers this year and there are 10,000 companies in the subsegment, it's big enough. If there are 20 companies, it's not.

### The word-of-mouth advantage

Tight subsegments have powerful internal communication networks. Industry conferences, trade publications, professional associations, and informal peer networks mean that success with one customer spreads quickly. The Janna Systems story shows this: once they became the CRM for investment banks, each new win made the next win easier because banks talked to each other.

### The leader's response

The biggest risk in Big Fish, Small Pond is that the market leader copies your subsegment solution once you've proven the market. Ideally, your advantage in the subsegment is based on capabilities the leader cannot easily replicate — particularly capabilities that would conflict with their positioning in the broader market. Coke can't make a product that's good for dogs without confusing their human customers. That's the moat.

### Starting narrow, expanding later

Big Fish, Small Pond does not mean staying small forever. It means starting narrow to get traction, then expanding once you have a proven base. Common expansion paths:
- Start with one industry vertical, expand to adjacent verticals (credit unions → retail banks → insurance)
- Start with one geography, expand to others
- Start with one company size, expand up or down market
- Start with a specialized product, expand to a platform once you own the niche

## Create a New Game in depth

### What you are claiming

"This problem is distinct enough, and the existing solutions fall short enough, that buyers need an entirely new category to make sense of what we do and why it matters."

### When this is genuinely necessary

New categories form when three things happen simultaneously:
1. An enabling technology shift makes something newly possible
2. Customer behavior or priorities shift in a way that makes a new solution relevant
3. No existing category captures the combination of capabilities and value

Eloqua's "marketing automation" category required: the rise of digital marketing (technology), the proliferation of demand generation teams (behavior shift), and the gap between "email marketing" and the broader workflow automation they provided.

### The education burden

Creating a new category means doing the following work in sequence, simultaneously:
1. Educate buyers that the problem is real and costly
2. Define what a solution to this problem should look like (this means defining the purchase criteria — which you will obviously define to favor your product)
3. Prove you are the best vendor in this new category

All three of these require sustained, consistent communication over a long period. This is expensive. Most startups underestimate the timeline and run out of money or patience before the category takes hold.

### The fast-follower risk

The most dangerous outcome of successful category creation: you do all the education work and a larger, better-resourced competitor swoops in at the moment buyers start to understand the category. This is why category creation usually requires either deep pockets or a very defensible lead time.

### The minimum conditions

Before recommending this style, confirm:
- The product genuinely cannot fit in any existing category without the comparison working against it
- There is a clear, articulable answer to "Why now? What changed that made this category possible/necessary?"
- The founders have explicitly committed to a multi-year, resource-intensive market education program
- Investors understand and support this approach

## Trend layering decision tree

```
Does a current trend exist that is relevant to your target customers?
    ↓ Yes
Does the trend directly reinforce your value themes?
    ↓ Yes
Do your best-fit customers already know and use this trend language?
    ↓ Yes
→ Use the trend. Layer it on top of the market category.
  Example: "[Category] + [Trend adjective]" (CRM → AI-powered CRM)

    ↓ No (customers don't use this language yet)
→ Use the trend selectively in content and thought leadership,
  not in primary positioning. Let customers come to it.

↓ No (trend doesn't reinforce value themes)
→ Do not use the trend. A clear boring position beats a confusing
  trendy one. State clearly: no trend layer is appropriate here.

↓ No trend exists that is relevant
→ Proceed without a trend. Many successful products don't need one.
```
