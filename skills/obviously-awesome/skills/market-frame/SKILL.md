# Market Frame Selection (Steps 8–9)

This skill executes Steps 8 and 9 of April Dunford's positioning process: choosing the market frame of reference that puts the product's strengths at the center, selecting the right positioning style, and optionally layering a relevant trend.

Read `references/positioning-styles.md` for detailed guidance on each of the three positioning styles before working through a specific product.

## Prerequisites

Value themes from Steps 5–7 must be defined before selecting a market frame. The frame is chosen to make those themes obvious — you cannot pick the right frame without knowing what you are trying to make obvious.

If value themes are not yet defined, use the `unique-value-mapping` skill first.

## What a market frame does

A market category is a frame of reference that triggers a set of assumptions in the customer's mind about competitors, features, and pricing. Choosing the right one is like choosing the right lighting for a photo: the subject doesn't change, but what the viewer perceives changes completely.

When you say "CRM," customers immediately assume Salesforce is the competitor, contact/opportunity tracking is the core feature, and per-seat monthly pricing is the norm. All those assumptions arrive without you saying a word. If those assumptions work in your favor, the category is doing marketing for you. If they work against you, you are spending every sales call fighting them.

**The goal:** Find the market category where your value themes are the expected and obvious strengths, not the surprising exceptions.

## How to select the market frame

### Method 1: Abductive reasoning

Ask: "What types of products typically have these features and deliver this value?"

List the value themes from Steps 5–7. For each one, ask: in what kind of product would a customer expect to find this capability? What market category assumes this kind of value as standard?

If multiple value themes point to the same category, that is a strong signal. If they point to different categories, ask which one best represents the dominant value the product delivers.

### Method 2: Adjacent market exploration

Look at markets adjacent to the current (default) category. Where do the lines blur? Which neighboring market makes the product's strengths obvious rather than surprising?

Pay particular attention to adjacent markets that are growing quickly. A rising tide of customer interest, analyst coverage, and media attention makes positioning in a growing category dramatically easier than fighting for attention in a mature, crowded one.

### Method 3: Customer language as signal

What words do best-fit customers use when they describe the product to colleagues? What do they search for when they found you? What category did they put the product in when they recommended it?

Customer language is a signal, but not a final answer. Customers anchor to whatever market they know and will often default to the most familiar adjacent category. Use it as a data point, not a directive.

## The three positioning styles

After identifying market frame candidates, choose the positioning style. The style determines how to compete within or against the chosen market.

### Style 1: Head to Head — win the whole market

**What it is:** You claim to be the best option for most or all buyers in an existing, defined market. You accept the current definition of the category and the current purchase criteria, and assert you are the best at delivering against them.

**When it works:**
- You are already the market leader and want to stay there
- The market exists and is well understood but no strong leader has emerged yet (so there is no incumbent to unseat)
- You have clear, demonstrable superiority on the criteria buyers already use to evaluate

**When it fails:**
- You are a small startup trying to beat an established leader who has more resources, more customers, and more credibility
- Your product is only better for some buyers, not most buyers

**The work:** Continuously reinforce the current buying criteria and prove you deliver them better than anyone. Defend against competitors who try to redefine those criteria.

---

### Style 2: Big Fish, Small Pond — own a subsegment

**What it is:** You carve a well-defined subsegment out of an existing market and build a clearly superior solution for that subsegment's specific, unmet needs. You do not fight the market leader for the whole market — you make them irrelevant for one clearly definable group of buyers.

**When it works:**
- A clear market leader exists that you cannot beat across the board
- An identifiable subsegment has needs that the leader does not address and is unlikely to address (because doing so would damage their positioning in the broader market)
- The subsegment is large enough to meet your near-term revenue goals
- You can name the subsegment in a way buyers in it immediately recognize

**When it fails:**
- The subsegment does not have genuinely different needs — just slightly different demographics
- The need is there but you are not significantly better than the leader for that group
- The subsegment is too small to build a sustainable business on

**The work:** Educate the subsegment about how the general-purpose solution is failing them specifically. Show a quantified gap. Prove you meet the baseline category criteria while delivering significantly more value for their specific needs.

The Janna Systems example: repositioned from "enterprise CRM" (losing to Siebel) to "CRM for investment banks" (where their relationship-mapping feature was transformatively valuable). Revenue went from $2M to $70M in 18 months.

---

### Style 3: Create a New Game — define a new category

**What it is:** You create a new market category because no existing one puts your strengths at the center. You first convince the market that this category needs to exist, then define what good looks like within it, then prove you are the leader.

**When it works:**
- Your product genuinely cannot be well positioned in any existing category without significant mismatch
- A major enabling shift has occurred (new technology, regulatory change, behavior shift) that makes a new category both possible and necessary
- You have the resources and patience for a long education cycle (typically years)
- You have investors who understand this is a long game

**When it fails:**
- You use it because you want to "be different" rather than because the positioning logic demands it
- Your product could actually fit into an existing category — it is just more exciting to say you are creating one
- You do not have the resources to sustain the market education effort
- A well-funded fast follower takes the category you created

**The work:** Sell the problem and the category first, your product second. Every piece of content, every sales conversation, every conference talk is about why this category must exist and what criteria buyers should use to evaluate it. Only then do you prove you are the best vendor.

The Eloqua example: Created "marketing automation" as a category out of "demand generation automation." Took years, but once the category took hold, they owned it and exited at $870M.

## Step 9: Layer on a trend (optional but often powerful)

A trend is not a market category. It is a characteristic that happens to be new and important right now. The market category tells buyers what the product is. The trend tells them why it is important to pay attention today.

Think of three overlapping circles: product strengths, market context, and trend. The goal is the intersection.

**How to find a relevant trend:**
- What technology shifts are your target customers currently tracking? (AI, machine learning, blockchain, IoT, autonomous systems, privacy regulations, new data standards)
- What regulatory or compliance changes are forcing your customers to act?
- What competitive or economic shifts are making your customers' old approach unsustainable?

**The test:** Does the trend reinforce the positioning, or does it just sound cool? If you stripped the trend language from the pitch, would the core positioning still be clear? If yes, the trend is additive. If removing it causes confusion, the trend is load-bearing in a bad way — the positioning is too dependent on hype.

The Redgate example: Already the leader in database tools. Added "Database DevOps" as a trend layer. Result: 100% increase in inbound leads, customers buying multiple products at once.

**Common mistakes:**
- Describing only the trend without first stating the market category. "The sharing economy for pets" tells you nothing. "Marketplace for pet services, powered by sharing economy principles" works.
- Using trend language that your customers don't recognize or use. If your buyers haven't heard of the trend, it creates confusion not urgency.
- Rebranding entirely around the trend when it's still emerging. Long Blockchain changed its name from Long Island Iced Tea — stock soared briefly, then collapsed when no real blockchain strategy existed.

## Output format

Deliver:

**1. Market frame recommendation**

```
Recommended market category: [category name]
Rationale: [why this category puts the value themes at the center]
Assumptions this category triggers: [what customers will assume about competitors, features, pricing]
How those assumptions work for us: [which assumptions are favorable]
How those assumptions work against us: [which assumptions need to be addressed]
```

**2. Positioning style recommendation**

```
Recommended style: [Head to Head / Big Fish Small Pond / Create a New Game]
Rationale: [why this style fits the competitive situation]
Subsegment (if Big Fish Small Pond): [specific subsegment definition]
Category to create (if New Game): [new category name and why it deserves to exist]
```

**3. Alternative market frames considered**

Brief note on 1–2 alternatives that were considered and rejected, with explanation.

**4. Trend layer (if applicable)**

```
Relevant trend: [trend name]
Link to positioning: [specific connection to the product's strengths]
How to use it: [suggested language or framing]
Warning (if applicable): [any risk of the trend creating confusion or dependency]
```

If no relevant trend applies cleanly, state that explicitly. A clear, boring position beats a baffling, trendy one.
